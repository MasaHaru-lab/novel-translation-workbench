# Gold Examples (Taste Anchors)

This file collects positive taste anchors: short source fragments paired with renderings that match the project's literary instinct on this book.

## What this file is

- A small, growing set of **example pairs** (source → preferred English rendering) that capture the *feel* of a correct translation choice.
- Each entry includes a one-line note on **why it works** and, when useful, what kind of weaker rendering it avoids.

## What this file is *not*

- Not a glossary. Glossary entries are stable mappings; gold examples are situational.
- Not a style rule. Style notes (`4. style_notes.md`) state principles; gold examples show what those principles look like landed in real prose.
- Not a bad-cases list. Bad-case notes catalogue what to avoid; gold examples show what to aim for.
- Not a global find-and-replace. The same source phrase in a different register, speaker, or scene may legitimately render differently.

Treat each entry as a **taste anchor**, not a rigid rule. When a future passage's situation matches, the gold rendering should be a strong default — but the translator/reviewer is still responsible for fit.

## When to consult this file

- During Prompt B review: if a phrase or line feels close to a recorded anchor, check whether the candidate rendering matches the anchor's instinct.
- When a previously translated phrase reappears and the context is genuinely similar.
- When proposing a "creative" rendering that drifts from a recorded anchor — confirm the drift is intentional, not accidental.

If a candidate rendering reaches the same effect through a different surface form, that is allowed. Anchors define the *target sensibility*, not the only acceptable wording.

## Update rule

- Add an entry only when a rendering clearly captures the right feel and you'd want future agents to see it.
- Keep each entry short: source fragment, preferred rendering, one-line "why", optional one-line "weak rendering avoided".
- If a later, stronger rendering supersedes an entry, update in place rather than accumulating near-duplicates.
- If two entries genuinely conflict, move the unresolved tension to `5. unresolved_decisions.md` and leave a pointer here.

## Entries

### 1. `啧啧暗叹`

- **Source:** `啧啧暗叹`
- **Preferred rendering:** *clicked his tongue inwardly* (or *gave a silent click of the tongue* in close-narrated contexts)
- **Why it works:** preserves the small, involuntary, almost-audible quality of the gesture, and keeps it interior — the character is reacting to himself, not performing for anyone. The physical *click* carries the narrator's wry texture without commentary.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** flattening to *sighed inwardly*, *muttered to himself*, or *shook his head in admiration* — these lose the specific tongue-click image and replace texture with generic interior reaction.

### 2. `你长得有点丑，不像我和你弟弟。`

- **Source:** `你长得有点丑，不像我和你弟弟。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *You're a bit ugly. Not like me and your brother.*
- **Why it works:** keeps the bluntness intact, lets the second sentence land as its own small blow, and trusts the reader to feel the cruelty without softening modifiers. The short, plain English mirrors the unembellished Chinese, and the line break between clauses preserves the casual, almost throwaway delivery that makes the cut sharper.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** smoothing to *You're not as good-looking as your brother and me*, *You're a little plain compared to us*, or any version that hedges *丑* into "plain" / "not as handsome". The point of the line is the bluntness; softening it neutralizes the scene.
