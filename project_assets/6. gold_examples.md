# Gold Examples (Taste Anchors)

This file collects positive taste anchors: short source fragments paired with renderings that match the project's literary instinct on this book.

## What this file is

- A small, growing set of **example pairs** (source → preferred English rendering) that capture the *feel* of a correct translation choice.
- Each entry includes a one-line note on **why it works** and, when useful, what kind of weaker rendering it avoids.

## What this file is *not*

- Not a glossary. Glossary entries are stable mappings; gold examples are situational.
- Not a style rule. Style notes (`4. style_notes.md`) state principles; gold examples show what those principles look like landed in real prose.
- Not a bad-cases list. Bad-case notes catalogue what to avoid; gold examples show what to aim for.
- Not a global find-and-replace. The same source phrase in a different register, speaker, or scene may legitimately render differently.

Treat each entry as a **taste anchor**, not a rigid rule. When a future passage's situation matches, the gold rendering should be a strong default — but the translator/reviewer is still responsible for fit.

## When to consult this file

- During Prompt B review: if a phrase or line feels close to a recorded anchor, check whether the candidate rendering matches the anchor's instinct.
- When a previously translated phrase reappears and the context is genuinely similar.
- When proposing a "creative" rendering that drifts from a recorded anchor — confirm the drift is intentional, not accidental.

If a candidate rendering reaches the same effect through a different surface form, that is allowed. Anchors define the *target sensibility*, not the only acceptable wording.

## Update rule

- Add an entry only when a rendering clearly captures the right feel and you'd want future agents to see it.
- Keep each entry short: source fragment, preferred rendering, one-line "why", optional one-line "weak rendering avoided".
- If a later, stronger rendering supersedes an entry, update in place rather than accumulating near-duplicates.
- If two entries genuinely conflict, move the unresolved tension to `5. unresolved_decisions.md` and leave a pointer here.

## Entries

### 1. `啧啧暗叹`

- **Source:** `啧啧暗叹`
- **Preferred rendering:** *clicked his tongue inwardly* (or *gave a silent click of the tongue* in close-narrated contexts)
- **Why it works:** preserves the small, involuntary, almost-audible quality of the gesture, and keeps it interior — the character is reacting to himself, not performing for anyone. The physical *click* carries the narrator's wry texture without commentary.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** flattening to *sighed inwardly*, *muttered to himself*, or *shook his head in admiration* — these lose the specific tongue-click image and replace texture with generic interior reaction.

### 2. `你长得有点丑，不像我和你弟弟。`

- **Source:** `你长得有点丑，不像我和你弟弟。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *You're a bit ugly. Not like me and your brother.*
- **Acceptable variant:** *You're rather ugly. Not like me and your brother.* — preserves bluntness; "rather" is a slightly stronger hedge than "a bit" but still lands. This variant is also the form echoed back in chapter 3 as a refrain (`Rather Ugly.`), so within this book it is in-canon.
- **Why it works:** keeps the bluntness intact, lets the second sentence land as its own small blow, and trusts the reader to feel the cruelty without softening modifiers. The short, plain English mirrors the unembellished Chinese, and the line break between clauses preserves the casual, almost throwaway delivery that makes the cut sharper.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** smoothing to *You're not as good-looking as your brother and me*, *You're a little plain compared to us*, or any version that hedges *丑* into "plain" / "not as handsome". The point of the line is the bluntness; softening it neutralizes the scene. Also avoid *Nothing like me or your brother* for the second clause — it over-hardens 不像 into a categorical denial, where the Chinese is a quieter comparative.

### 3. Daoist-perception 黑气 (death/ill omen on a face)

- **Source pattern:** `脸上的黑气更重了` / `面上隐隐有黑气` and similar — a Daoist-aware character perceiving an aura on a body.
- **Preferred rendering:** *the death-pall on her face had deepened* / *a darkening sign clung to her face*. Choose the English noun by what the perception **means** in the scene (death, illness, anger, ill omen).
- **Why it works:** treats the term as a perception of meaning, not as a physical phenomenon. The English reader receives the omen the way the observing character does — as a sign — without the literal-vapor image that breaks the world.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *the black vapor on her face had grown heavier*, *black gas/steam/smoke around her face*. These are calques of the morpheme rather than the function and read as converted text.

### 4. Compact interior compound (e.g. `不满与莫名烦躁`)

- **Source pattern:** Two or three short interior-state nouns joined into one compound feeling.
- **Preferred rendering:** name the dominant feeling in one short clause and let the secondary shading sit as a verb, adverb, or follow-on beat — e.g. *her brows knit, displeased and obscurely irritated.* Two beats; not three.
- **Why it works:** matches the source's compactness. The unsorted, half-named quality of the feeling is part of the characterization; preserving it preserves the texture.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *her brows knitting together with a mix of dissatisfaction and confused irritation* / *a complicated blend of X and Y* — three or more modifiers piled on one noun. Reads as over-translated; explains a feeling the source deliberately leaves un-explained.

### 5. Chapter-title refrain (e.g. `雪上加霜` re-appearing inside the chapter)

- **Source pattern:** the chapter title (or near-restatement) reappears inside the chapter as a short narrative refrain — often a single phrase or interior thought at a moment of recognition.
- **Preferred rendering:** isolate it as its own short paragraph, in **Title Case with a terminal period and no quotation marks** — e.g. *Adding insult to injury.* on its own line. Title Case + period applies even when the source uses sentence-final `！` or wraps the line in 引号; the refrain is narrative callback, not spoken dialogue, so source `！` and quote marks do not carry over.
- **Why it works:** preserves the structural callback. The refrain is doing the same work the chapter title does, and isolating it (and casing it like a heading) lets the reader feel the echo.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *adding insult to injury* buried inline mid-paragraph, lowercase, with no separation. Loses the refrain function and reads like a stray idiom.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** rendering the refrain as quoted dialogue with sentence case and an exclamation mark — e.g. `"Rather ugly!"` for the ch3 echo of gold #2. The quotes reframe a narrative callback as a spoken line; the lowercase + `!` reframes a heading-style refrain as ordinary speech. Entry #2's `Rather Ugly.` is this anchor applied to the ch1 cruel-line echo in ch3 and is the canonical surface form for that refrain.
