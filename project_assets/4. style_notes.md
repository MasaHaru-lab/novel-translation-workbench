# Style Notes

## Core prose target

- The English should read like real fiction, not like converted text.
- Aim for controlled, readable, literary prose rather than flashy writing.
- Natural English is required, but unwarranted invention is not allowed.

## Information boundaries

- Do not add facts that are not supported by the Chinese source.
- Do not casually add ownership, possessives, emotional force, or causal explanation.
- Do not over-assign gender when the source does not clearly establish it.
- Preserve ambiguity when the source is ambiguous, unless English readability truly requires light clarification.

## Tone control

- Do not over-dramatize.
- Do not add sarcasm, tenderness, cruelty, fear, warmth, or intensity unless the source supports it.
- Do not perform the line beyond what the Chinese earns.

## Register

- Avoid overly modern, chatty, or sitcom-like English in historical, court, clan-household, or semi-historical settings.
- Prefer controlled, neutral, slightly formal phrasing when needed to preserve atmosphere.
- Do not flatten social hierarchy or interpersonal pressure.

## Title epithet format

- Chinese "XX老道 / XX大师 / XX真人 / XX道长 / XX师父" style epithets default to "XX + Title" in English, e.g. 赤元老道 → Chi Yuan Daoist.
- Do not drift into "the old Daoist XX" constructions, appositive variants ("XX, the Daoist"), or descriptive rewrites unless a specific local context clearly requires it.
- Keep stable through glossary / title memory and enforce during review passes.

## Narrative vs dialogue

- Narration should be smooth, controlled, and readable.
- Dialogue should preserve character differences in status, temperament, education, and attitude.
- Do not make every character sound like the same neutral modern narrator.

## Action beats

- Keep simple actions simple.
- Do not add ornamental body language, cinematic gesture, or reaction beats unless supported by the source.

## Revision discipline

- If the prose becomes smoother but less faithful, pull it back.
- If the wording becomes more stylish but less true to the scene, pull it back.
- If a line sounds vivid in English but breaks the world of the novel, pull it back.

## Facial-color / emotional direction

Preserve the emotional direction of 脸色黑了 / 白了 / 青了 etc. 黑了 usually means the face darkened with anger or displeasure, not "turned pale." Do not reverse the emotional signal. When uncertain, check the surrounding scene pressure rather than defaulting to the most familiar English equivalent.

## Narrative stance

Preserve the source's narrative stance. Do not add rhetorical defenses, psychological explanations, or causal framing that the source does not contain. A factual narration of events should not be rewritten into a defensive or sympathetic framing. If the source states what happened without evaluation, the translation should do the same.

## Address and title consistency

Do not switch between different English renderings of the same character or location title within a single chapter. Each entity should have one stable rendering per chapter:

- If a character is introduced as "Doctor Zhang," do not later call him "Zhang Dafu" or "Dr. Zhang" in the same chapter. Pick one canonical rendering and stay with it.
- If a location is introduced as "Qingping Temple," do not later call it "Qingping Abbey." The reader infers different places.
- If a character is addressed as "Temple Master" in dialogue, do not switch to "Abbess" for that same speaker and same character in the same chapter.
- The same applies to supplementary characters: "Doctor Yang" should not become "Dr. Yang" or "Yang Dafu" mid-chapter.

When establishing a rendering for a new character or place:
- Prefer the pattern already established in project assets (e.g. "Doctor [Surname]" for physicians, matching "Lady [Surname]" for married women, "[Name] Daoist" for Daoists).
- Do not mix transliteration (Zhang Dafu) with functional title (Doctor Zhang) for the same person. Make a system choice and keep it.
- If the source itself varies the form of address (张大夫 spoken by narrator vs 张老大夫 by a subordinate), translate the variation in register without switching the base rendering.

## Game-rank and internet-culture idioms

Chinese terms from gaming, internet slang, or modern competitive culture (e.g. 青铜 meaning "low rank/novice," 王者 meaning "top-tier/ace," 青铜/王者 as a contrast between amateur and expert) must be translated by cultural function, not by surface literal meaning:

- Identify the real-world function: 青铜 here signals "amateur/low-ranked," 王者 signals "top-tier/expert." Translate that function.
- Do not render 青铜 as literal "bronze" when the context is about skill ranking or perceived incompetence. This produces awkward English ("they thought she was bronze") that readers without Chinese gaming culture will find opaque.
- Prefer natural English equivalents that preserve the contrast: "They had taken her for a novice, but she was a master," or "they thought they were dealing with an amateur — turns out she was the expert." Match the scene's register (casual/serious/humorous).
- If the term is embedded in dialogue (e.g. a character using gaming slang), consider whether that slang would plausibly exist in the character's world. If not, translate the comparative function rather than the gaming term.
- When in doubt, prefer clarity of the scene's point (novice vs expert, amateur vs ace) over literal fidelity to the gaming metaphor.

## Technical exposition in fiction prose

Medical, Daoist, alchemical, formula, or other specialized explanations (symptom lists, formula breakdowns, procedural descriptions) must remain accurate but must not read as textbook exposition:

- Do not enumerate clinical symptom lists in flat narrative voice ("vomiting blood, bloody stools, sudden chills and shivering accompanied by high fever, a crimson tongue, a thin, rapid, barely perceptible pulse"). This stops the fiction and switches register to a medical textbook.
- Embed specialized information into scene action: a character listing symptoms while examining a patient, a physician mentally running through a formula, dialogue where one character explains to another.
- Preserve the information fidelity but reshape the delivery so it stays in the fictional register: shorter phrases, embedded in observation, varied sentence rhythm.
- Character knowledge should sound like that character thinking or speaking, not like a reference work narrating at the reader.
- This is most important for long runs of technical content (formula reasoning, diagnosis, ritual procedure). Break them into scene beats with character reactions, interruptions, or physical actions.
- If the source itself is a dense block of technical exposition, the translator's job is to find the scene within it — do not simply convert the Chinese technical terms into English technical terms and call it done.
