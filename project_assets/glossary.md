# Glossary

## Working glossary

### 记在名下
- Chinese: 记在名下
- Working English rendering: registered under [someone's] name
- Notes: Keep the formal household / lineage sense. Do not flatten into casual family phrasing.

### 生养之恩
- Chinese: 生养之恩
- Working English rendering: the debt of giving birth and raising [someone]
- Notes: Context-sensitive. Preserve the layered sense of obligation and upbringing.

### 抚养之恩
- Chinese: 抚养之恩
- Working English rendering: the debt of raising [someone]
- Notes: Related to, but not identical with, 生养之恩.

### 冲煞
- Chinese: 冲煞
- Working English rendering: clashing baleful influence
- Notes: Provisional. Needs stable handling across fate / Daoist / metaphysical language.

### 命格
- Chinese: 命格
- Working English rendering: fate pattern
- Notes: Prefer controlled metaphysical wording; do not make it sound like modern self-help language.

### 清贵
- Chinese: 清贵
- Working English rendering: noble refinement
- Notes: Provisional. Preserve status and moral-social texture; avoid flat modern phrasing.

### 太庙祭祀
- Chinese: 太庙祭祀
- Working English rendering: ancestral temple rites
- Notes: Prefer a stable ritual-language rendering across the book.

### 三牲
- Chinese: 三牲
- Working English rendering: sacrificial triad / the three sacrificial animals
- Notes: Ritual term for the three traditional sacrificial animals (ox, sheep, pig). Prefer concise rendering; avoid enumerating the three unless prose context clearly requires it. Do not paraphrase loosely.

### 七窍流血
- Chinese: 七窍流血
- Working English rendering: bleeding from the seven orifices
- Notes: Keep vivid but controlled. Do not embellish.

### 吹枕头风
- Chinese: 吹枕头风
- Working English rendering: whisper in the emperor's ear
- Notes: Translate by function in running prose, not literally.

### 平反
- Chinese: 平反
- Working English rendering: clear the family name
- Notes: In context, may need slight variation, but preserve the sense of formal reversal of injustice.

### 圣眷
- Chinese: 圣眷
- Working English rendering: imperial favor
- Notes: Keep stable. Do not over-modernize.

### 大灃
- Chinese: 大灃
- Pinyin: Dà Fēng
- Working English rendering: Great Feng
- Notes: Fictional dynasty name. 灃 = fēng, not hóng. Never render as "Great Hong" or "Great Hong Dynasty." Use consistently once stabilized.

### 福礼 / 福了一礼
- Chinese: 福礼 / 福了一礼
- Working English rendering: gave a formal feminine salute / made a formal salute
- Notes: Historical feminine ritual gesture (hands clasped at the side), not a generic Western bow. Do not flatten to "bowed." In running prose, wording may vary but must preserve the ritual-gesture sense.

### 官家出身
- Chinese: 官家出身
- Working English rendering: from an official household / from a family of officials
- Notes: Means the family held civil-service/official positions. Do not elevate to "noble lineage" or "aristocratic." The Qin family are officials, not hereditary nobles. Distinguish from noble/aristocratic status.

### 青铜 (game-rank / skill-level metaphor)
- Chinese: 青铜
- Pinyin: qīngtóng
- Register guidance (no fixed canonical — pick by context): amateur, novice, low-ranked player, or "they thought she was nothing"
- Notes: In Chinese internet and gaming culture, 青铜 (bronze) is the lowest competitive tier. When used as a metaphor in fiction (她是青铜？是王者), it contrasts perceived low skill with actual high skill. Do NOT render as literal bronze — this produces opaque English. Translate the comparative function: novice vs expert, amateur vs ace, they thought she was nothing / they had dismissed her. Context determines the natural English equivalent. This is guidance, not a substitution rule.

### 王者 (game-rank / skill-level metaphor)
- Chinese: 王者
- Pinyin: wángzhě
- Register guidance (no fixed canonical — pick by context): top-tier, ace, master, or champion
- Notes: The counterpart to 青铜 in ranking metaphors. Indicates the highest tier of skill or status. When paired contrastively with 青铜, render the pair consistently — pick one register (novice vs ace, amateur vs master) and keep it. Do NOT render as literal king unless the context is explicitly about royalty, not gaming ranks. This is guidance, not a substitution rule.

### 吉祥物
- Chinese: 吉祥物
- Working English rendering: auspicious creature / auspicious symbol / blessed symbol
- Notes: In founding-myth, dynastic, or ritual contexts, avoid modern "mascot" or "lucky mascot." Prefer historical-register wording that preserves the ritual/symbolic weight.

### 雪上加霜
- Chinese: 雪上加霜
- Working English rendering: adding insult to injury
- Notes: Common Chinese idiom meaning "frost on top of snow" — a bad situation gets worse. The canonical English idiomatic equivalent is "adding insult to injury." Do not render literally.

### 屋漏偏逢连夜雨
- Chinese: 屋漏偏逢连夜雨
- Working English rendering: Misfortune never comes alone.
- Notes: Misfortune stacking relentlessly with no respite. Do not conflate with 雪上加霜 (adding insult to injury) — 雪上加霜 marks a single second blow making things worse; 屋漏偏逢连夜雨 marks continuous piling-on. See gold example #7.

### 节节开花
- Chinese: 节节开花
- Working English rendering: success at every turn / won battle after battle
- Notes: Literally "bloom at every joint." Match register to scene: martial contexts → "won battle after battle" / "swept from one victory to the next"; general narrative → "success at every turn." Do not render literally as "blossomed section by section." The through-line is continuous, unimpeded progress — the plant-growth image does not need to survive.

### 人伢子
- Chinese: 人伢子
- Working English rendering: servant broker / household-staff broker
- Notes: In historical Chinese fiction, a broker who arranges the sale or hire of household servants. Do NOT use "slave trader" — that carries modern abolitionist weight and over-dramatizes the transaction. The source treats these as routine household management.

### 该营业了
- Chinese: 该营业了
- Working English rendering: time to get to work / time to start earning
- Notes: Colloquial, wry register — a reminder that someone needs to start conducting business. In historical settings, prefer 'time to get to work' or 'time to start earning.' Avoid overly modern phrasing ('open for business,' 'starting the shift').

### 红眼皮
- Chinese: 红眼皮
- Working English rendering: covetous eyes / greedy stare
- Notes: Figurative idiom for envy or covetousness. Do NOT translate literally as "red eyelids." The image signals desire, not a physical description of the eyelid. Prefer "covetous eyes" or "greedy stare" depending on context.

### 扎穴位
- Chinese: 扎穴位
- Working English rendering: insert needles into acupoints
- Notes: Acupuncture procedure. Use "acupoints" not "pressure points" (martial arts terminology). Keep stable in medical scenes.

### 当局者迷旁观者清
- Chinese: 当局者迷旁观者清
- Working English rendering: The outsider sees most of the game.
- Notes: Standard English equivalent for "onlookers see more than players." Do NOT render literally as "The onlooker sees the game best." Keep natural for fiction prose.

### 狐媚
- Chinese: 狐媚
- Working English rendering: seductress / dangerously alluring / fox-spirit cunning
- Notes: Strong pejorative implying improper, morally dangerous seduction. Do not soften to merely 'alluring' or 'charming.' When paired with 德不配位 ('unworthy of rank'), preserve the full moral condemnation — this is a character accusation, not a compliment.

### 味如嚼蜡
- Chinese: 味如嚼蜡
- Working English rendering: was like chewing on wax / was like sawdust in the mouth
- Notes: Food was tasteless and joyless. Do not translate literally as "tasted like chewing wax" — the copula form "was like chewing on wax" flows more naturally. Choose 'wax' or 'sawdust' by register; both are idiomatic.

### 吃饱了撑的
- Chinese: 吃饱了撑的
- Working English rendering: eaten your fill and come looking for trouble
- Notes: Idiom implying someone has too much leisure and starts unnecessary trouble. Preserve the blunt, dismissive register.

### 太素脉法
- Chinese: 太素脉法
- Working English rendering: the Taisu pulse method
- Notes: Fictional Daoist pulse diagnostic technique. Do not render as bare pinyin without gloss. Canonical rendering: "the Taisu pulse method" or "her Taisu pulse method." Do not over-explain. Note: pinyin is Tàisū — render as "Taisu", not "Taishu".

### 死马当活马医
- Chinese: 死马当活马医
- Working English rendering: treat it as a last resort / try anything when all hope seems lost
- Notes: Idiom meaning "try anything when all hope seems lost" (lit. "treat a dead horse as if it were still alive"). Do NOT translate literally — the image does not survive into English fiction. Prefer "it was a last resort," "they had no other choice," or equivalent natural phrasing.

### 强心针 (metaphorical)
- Chinese: 强心针
- Working English rendering: reviving tonic / cordial / reviving draft
- Notes: Literally "cardiac stimulant injection." When used metaphorically, prefer historical-register equivalents ('reviving tonic,' 'cordial'). Do NOT use "shot of adrenaline" — anachronistic in a historical/fantasy setting. In literal medical contexts, keep the medical sense.

### 惊惧莫名
- Chinese: 惊惧莫名
- Working English rendering: a nameless terror / a terror beyond words
- Notes: Fear that cannot be named or understood. Stronger than 'inexplicable fear.' Prefer 'nameless terror' for vividness.

### 幸灾乐祸
- Chinese: 幸灾乐祸
- Working English rendering: glee at another's misfortune / barely concealed delight
- Notes: Do not use modern loanwords like "schadenfreude." In affectionate/teasing contexts prefer 'barely concealed glee' or 'wry delight.' In more cutting contexts, 'gloating.' Match register to scene.

### 缺心眼
- Chinese: 缺心眼
- Working English rendering: oblivious / simple-minded / naive
- Notes: Mild, often affectionate-to-contemptuous descriptor for someone who lacks awareness or common sense. Not as harsh as "stupid" or "thick-headed." Register ranges from wry family complaint (oblivious ancestor) to sharper criticism. Do not use "thick-headed" — that reads more insulting than the source.

### 一刻钟
- Chinese: 一刻钟
- Working English rendering: a quarter hour
- Notes: Precisely 15 minutes. Do NOT render as "an hour," "the first hour," or any other time unit. One quarter of an ancient Chinese 时辰. Fixed measurement — no paraphrase.

### 嗔怪
- Chinese: 嗔怪
- Working English rendering: fond reproach / playful scolding
- Notes: Mock-blame or affectionate scolding between close relations. Register is warm and intimate, not cold or serious. Do not render as "mild reproach" or plain "reproach" — those strip the doting quality. Reserve for contexts of intimacy and familiarity.

### 神府 / 神府识台
- Chinese: 神府 / 神府识台
- Working English rendering: spiritual palace / spiritual palace and consciousness
- Notes: Cultivation term. 神府 = inner spiritual space (dantian). Do not render as "divine residence" — that is a calque. 识台 = platform of consciousness. In cultivation-register prose, prefer "spiritual palace" or "dantian" over "divine residence."

### 不干人事
- Chinese: 不干人事
- Working English rendering: have no humanity / without the human heart
- Notes: Complaint idiom for someone who acts unreasonably or callously, as if devoid of human feeling. The emphasis is on the *absence of humanity*, not merely behavioral non-conformity. Preferred rendering leans lyrical: "gods have no humanity," "without the beautiful human heart." Do not collapse to "not generous" or "not fair" — those miss the humaneness dimension entirely.

### 人算不如天算
- Chinese: 人算不如天算
- Working English rendering: man proposes, heaven disposes
- Notes: Standard English proverbial equivalent. Do NOT render literally ('man's calculations can't match heaven's plan') — reads as converted text. Use in both dialogue and narrative.

### 自觉
- Chinese: 自觉
- Working English rendering: conscientious / acting on one's own initiative
- Notes: When used in dialogue as a callback (e.g., 自觉性), prefer 'conscientious' or 'being conscientious.' Avoid 'self-aware' (different meaning). Important tonal marker for Liuxi Qin's character voice.

### 得寸进尺
- Chinese: 得寸进尺
- Working English rendering: take an inch and ask for a mile / push for more
- Notes: Idiom meaning 'to push for more after getting an advantage.' The canonical English equivalent is 'take an inch and ask for a mile.' Do not over-render as 'never satisfied' — too generic.

### 不求大师
- Chinese: 不求大师
- Working English rendering: Buqiu the Daoist
- Notes: Respectful address for the Daoist physician 不求. When used as direct address (不求大师), render as 'Buqiu the Daoist' — do NOT render as 'Master Buqiu.' The 大师 honorific is rendered as 'the Daoist' not 'Master' in this character's case, consistent with the Chi Yuan Daoist convention.

### 祖荫
- Chinese: 祖荫
- Working English rendering: accumulated virtue / blessings from virtue
- Notes: Ancestral merit / blessings that protect descendants. 'Blessings of your accumulated virtue will benefit your descendants.' Do NOT render as 'ancestral virtue' (awkward calque) or 'ancestral protection' (different concept).

### 鄙人
- Chinese: 鄙人
- Working English rendering: this humble one / I (with formal register cues)
- Notes: Humble self-reference used by deferential characters (e.g. Squire Qian addressing a Daoist). Do not flatten to plain 'I' in every instance. Prefer 'this humble one' for the first instance in a scene; vary naturally thereafter while preserving the deferential tone.

### 辰时
- Chinese: 辰时
- Working English rendering: the early morning hour / the seventh hour
- Notes: Traditional two-hour period (7–9 AM). Do NOT render as pinyin 'the hour of Chen' — opaque to English readers. In fiction, 'the early morning hour' is usually sufficient.

### 淡笑
- Chinese: 淡笑
- Working English rendering: a restrained smile / a composed smile
- Notes: A calm, controlled, slightly distant smile characteristic of Liuxi Qin's demeanor. Do NOT render as 'faint smile' — carries unintended connotations of weakness or fading. Prefer 'restrained' or 'composed' depending on scene register.

### 坐吃山空
- Chinese: 坐吃山空
- Working English rendering: eat without producing
- Notes: Idiom for consuming resources without generating income. 'Eat without producing' is a natural, concise English equivalent. Do not render literally as 'sit and eat the mountain empty.'

### 四色糕点
- Chinese: 四色糕点
- Working English rendering: four-colored pastries
- Notes: Four different varieties of pastries (not a translation of specific fillings). Keep as 'four-colored' if context allows; do not flatten to 'assorted pastries' unless variation is clearly required.

### 因果
- Chinese: 因果
- Working English rendering: karma / karmic law / karmic bond
- Notes: In this book's Daoist context, 因果 refers to karmic cause-and-effect, not mere causality. When used in narrative or dialogue by Daoist characters, prefer 'karma' or 'karmic bond' (see gold example #11). Avoid generic 'cause and effect' — too neutral, weakens world-building.

### 五弊三缺
- Chinese: 五弊三缺
- Working English rendering: the Five Calamities and Three Deficiencies
- Notes: Daoist concept — the price/punishment for practicing certain arts (divination, medicine, etc.). Capitalize as a term-of-art: 'the Five Calamities and Three Deficiencies.' Do not render lowercase or explain in-sentence unless context demands it.

### 不求
- Chinese: 不求
- Working English rendering: Buqiu
- Notes: Daoist sobriquet meaning 'not seeking.' Used as a proper name for a Daoist physician character. Do not translate literally as 'not seeking' in dialogue titles. In narrative gloss, may contextualize as 'the Daoist physician called Buqiu.' Treat as a proper name throughout.

### 好奇害死猫
- Chinese: 好奇害死猫
- Working English rendering: Curiosity leads to trouble. / Curiosity is dangerous.
- Notes: A modern Chinese borrowing of the English 'curiosity killed the cat.' Do NOT render back as 'curiosity killed the cat' — that re-imports a Western proverb into a historical/fantasy Chinese setting and breaks register. Use a neutral equivalent: 'curiosity leads to trouble' or 'curiosity is dangerous.'

### 金刚钻
- Chinese: 金刚钻
- Working English rendering: (figurative) the tool for the job / the skill to do the job
- Notes: From the idiom 没有金刚钻别揽瓷器活 — 'if you don't have the tool, don't take on the job.' When used assertively (我就是那金刚钻), the meaning is 'I have the skill and resources for any job.' Translate by function: 'I can do any job' / 'I have what it takes.' Do NOT render as 'diamond drill' — produces an opaque, modern-sounding image that breaks the historical setting.

### 黄绿大夫
- Chinese: 黄绿大夫
- Working English rendering: yellow-green quack
- Notes: Derogatory term for an incompetent physician (lit. 'yellow-green doctor'). Render as 'yellow-green quack' or bare 'quack' depending on register. Preserve the contemptuous, dismissive tone. Do not soften to 'unqualified doctor' or 'quack doctor.'

### 子女宫
- Chinese: 子女宫
- Working English rendering: children's-palace (area beneath the eyes, per physiognomy)
- Notes: Specialized face-reading term. When first used in a scene, gloss as 'children's-palace area beneath the eyes.' In later uses, may use bare 'children's palace' if context is clear. Do not flatten to generic facial descriptors.

### 收生奶奶
- Chinese: 收生奶奶
- Working English rendering: birth-attendant / ritual birth-midwife
- Notes: Honorific for the midwife who assists at childbirth rituals. 'Birth-attendant' preserves the honorific without being overly formal. Do not use bare 'midwife' — loses the ritual dimension. When performing the Third-Day Rite (洗三), the role has ceremonial duties beyond medical assistance.

### 法器
- Chinese: 法器
- Working English rendering: Daoist artifact / ritual implement
- Notes: A spiritually-charged object used in Daoist practice or cultivation. Do NOT render as 'mystical tool' (too fantasy-generic) or 'magical item' (loses Daoist specificity). Prefer 'Daoist artifact' for contextual clarity. When nurtured with the practitioner's energy, 'artifact Liuxi Qin had nurtured' or similar phrasing.

### 君要臣死，臣不得不死
- Chinese: 君要臣死，臣不得不死
- Working English rendering: when the ruler wants his minister dead, the minister has no choice but to die
- Notes: Classical maxim on absolute imperial authority over officials. Cited in this book in the context of political risk and official service. Preserve the parallelism and the sense of compulsion. Do not modernize. Variations on the base wording are acceptable if context requires ("if the ruler wills the death of his minister, the minister cannot refuse"), but keep the formal register and the doubled structure intact.

### 防我 (defensive guarding)
- Chinese: 防我 / 对方在防我
- Working English rendering: being guarded against [me] / on guard against [me]
- Notes: Indicates the other party is being defensive or keeping their guard up against the speaker. Do NOT render as 'keeping something from me' — that changes the register from defensive posture to information-withholding, which is a different interpersonal dynamic.

### 一盏茶功夫 / 一碗茶功夫 (time unit idiom)
- Chinese: 一盏茶功夫 / 一碗茶功夫
- Working English rendering: within the time it takes to drink a cup of tea / in less time than it takes to drink a cup of tea
- Notes: Classical Chinese time idiom meaning a very short period (roughly 5–15 minutes). Preferred over bare 'within minutes' (breaks historical register). Use 'within the time it takes to drink a cup of tea' or 'in less time than it takes to drink a cup of tea.' See gold examples #47.

### 大师 (when addressing a Daoist practitioner)
- Chinese: 大师
- English rendering: Daoist (as address) / the Daoist (as epithet)
- Notes: Honorific for a skilled Daoist practitioner (physician, ritual master). Do NOT render as 'Master' — this imports martial-arts cultivation-novel register that misaligns with the medical/ritual setting. Follow the established pattern: 赤元老道 → Chi Yuan Daoist; 不求大师 → Buqiu the Daoist. A bare 大师 in address resolves to 'Daoist' (e.g. 'Good day, Daoist'). See also characters.md §'Qi Qian' for the specific scene where this drift was first flagged.

### 泪堂
- Chinese: 泪堂
- Working English rendering: Tear Hall, the area beneath the eyes
- Notes: Physiognomy term for the area under the eyes, read for indicators of children/descendants and emotional state. On first use in a scene, gloss as 'Tear Hall, the area beneath her eyes.' Later mentions in the same scene may use bare 'Tear Hall' if context is clear. Do NOT render as 'tear ducts' (anatomical, breaks the divinatory register) or as bare 'eye area' (loses the named term-of-art). Adjacent face-reading concept: §'子女宫'.

### 寿不过甲
- Chinese: 寿不过甲
- Working English rendering: would not live past sixty / would not pass her sixtieth year
- Notes: 甲 here refers to a 60-year cycle (a full 甲子). The phrase is a fortune-telling / fate-pattern judgment, not a medical prognosis. Render as 'would not live past sixty' or 'would not pass her sixtieth year.' Do NOT collapse to bare 'would die soon' or 'short-lived' — flattens the divinatory specificity. Pair with §'命格' and §'太素脉法' for the divinatory register.
