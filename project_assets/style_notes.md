# Style Notes

## Core prose target

- The English should read like real fiction, not like converted text.
- Aim for controlled, readable, literary prose rather than flashy writing.
- Natural English is required, but unwarranted invention is not allowed.

## Information boundaries

- Do not add facts that are not supported by the Chinese source.
- Do not casually add ownership, possessives, emotional force, or causal explanation.
- Do not over-assign gender when the source does not clearly establish it.
- Preserve ambiguity when the source is ambiguous, unless English readability truly requires light clarification.

## Tone control — amplified emotion

Do not amplify or dramatize neutral narrative observations. When the source uses a neutral phrase (有几分复杂, 冷静叙事), do not expand it with invented emotional language ('stirred something complicated,' 'a detachment'). Transmit the same level of emotional color as the source — no more. If the Chinese is understated, the English must be understated too.

Be especially cautious with `别提多` constructions (别提多难看, 别提多生气 etc.). These are emphatic in Chinese but should not be rendered with added narrator commentary or wry spin. Prefer controlled observation: 'her expression was dark' over 'was grim indeed.'

## Tone control

- Do not over-dramatize.
- Do not add sarcasm, tenderness, cruelty, fear, warmth, or intensity unless the source supports it.
- Do not perform the line beyond what the Chinese earns.

## Register

- Avoid overly modern, chatty, or sitcom-like English in historical, court, clan-household, or semi-historical settings.
- Prefer controlled, neutral, slightly formal phrasing when needed to preserve atmosphere.
- Do not flatten social hierarchy or interpersonal pressure.

## Title epithet format

- Chinese "XX老道 / XX大师 / XX真人 / XX道长 / XX师父" style epithets default to "XX + Title" in English, e.g. 赤元老道 → Chi Yuan Daoist.
- Do not drift into "the old Daoist XX" constructions, appositive variants ("XX, the Daoist"), or descriptive rewrites unless a specific local context clearly requires it.
- Keep stable through glossary / title memory and enforce during review passes.

## Narrative vs dialogue

- Narration should be smooth, controlled, and readable.
- Dialogue should preserve character differences in status, temperament, education, and attitude.
- Do not make every character sound like the same neutral modern narrator.
- When a character speaks directly or bluntly in Chinese, preserve that bluntness in English. 丑 in "你长得有点丑" is a direct, unfiltered statement — do not soften it to "rather plain-looking" or equivalent hedged language. The source character's register choice (blunt, gentle, sarcastic, formal) is part of their voice and should survive translation. Do not sand away interpersonal pressure or blunt register in dialogue.

## Internal reaction and inner voice

Chinese prose often uses vivid onomatopoeic or exclamatory phrases for internal reactions (啧啧暗叹, 暗想, 暗自称奇, etc.). These carry the character's wry, amused, surprised, or appraising inner voice.

When translating:
- Do not flatten vivid internal reactions into neutral report language. Rendering 啧啧暗叹 as "made a silent appraisal" loses the wry, youthful, internally amused tone of the original.
- Look for English equivalents that preserve the character's inner register — a wry observation, a silent click of the tongue, an amused thought — rather than defaulting to generic narration.
- The character's internal voice is a material part of their personality. A neutral report that could belong to any character is usually a failed rendering.

## Action beats

- Keep simple actions simple.
- Do not add ornamental body language, cinematic gesture, or reaction beats unless supported by the source.

## Revision discipline

- If the prose becomes smoother but less faithful, pull it back.
- If the wording becomes more stylish but less true to the scene, pull it back.
- If a line sounds vivid in English but breaks the world of the novel, pull it back.

## Facial-color / emotional direction

Preserve the emotional direction of 脸色黑了 / 白了 / 青了 / 绿了 etc. 黑了 usually means the face darkened with anger or displeasure, not "turned pale." 青了 / 绿了 indicates shock, bitterness, or extreme displeasure — not merely physical pallor. Do not reverse or soften the emotional signal. If the source uses a color idiom (e.g., 脸都绿了), translate the emotional impact, not a literal color description. Near-equivalents: 'face darkened' (黑了), 'face went waxen' / 'face went green' (青/绿), 'turned pale' (白了). Do not replace with weak alternatives like 'expression flickered' or 'just a flicker of distress.' When uncertain, check the surrounding scene pressure rather than defaulting to the most familiar English equivalent.

## Narrative stance

Preserve the source's narrative stance. Do not add rhetorical defenses, psychological explanations, or causal framing that the source does not contain. A factual narration of events should not be rewritten into a defensive or sympathetic framing. If the source states what happened without evaluation, the translation should do the same.

## Address and title consistency

Do not switch between different English renderings of the same character or location title within a single chapter. Each entity should have one stable rendering per chapter:

- If a character is introduced as "Doctor Zhang," do not later call him "Zhang Dafu" or "Dr. Zhang" in the same chapter. Pick one canonical rendering and stay with it.
- If a location is introduced as "Qingping Temple," do not later call it "Qingping Abbey." The reader infers different places.
- If a character is addressed as "Temple Master" in dialogue, do not switch to "Abbess" for that same speaker and same character in the same chapter.
- The same applies to supplementary characters: "Doctor Yang" should not become "Dr. Yang" or "Yang Dafu" mid-chapter.

When establishing a rendering for a new character or place:
- Prefer the pattern already established in project assets (e.g. "Doctor [Surname]" for physicians, matching "Lady [Surname]" for married women, "[Name] Daoist" for Daoists).
- Do not mix transliteration (Zhang Dafu) with functional title (Doctor Zhang) for the same person. Make a system choice and keep it.
- If the source itself varies the form of address (张大夫 spoken by narrator vs 张老大夫 by a subordinate), translate the variation in register without switching the base rendering.

## Chinese household-address terms (XX家的 / X房的)

Chinese clan-household address terms (老二家的, 老大家的, 大房的, 二房的, 三房的) identify a person by their husband's or father's rank within the family hierarchy. These are socially-situated labels used in dialogue, not clan-structure exposition.

When translating:
- Do NOT render these literally ("Second Branch," "Eldest Branch's wife"). This produces unnatural English fiction that reads like converted text.
- Recognize them as dialogue-role labels and recast the sentence naturally: use the character's name (Lady Xie), a relational description, or restructure the sentence to avoid the label entirely.
- The English reader does not need the clan-branch label preserved verbatim to follow the scene's social dynamics — the surrounding dialogue and context establish who is being addressed. When a senior family member addresses someone by their household position, the natural English equivalent is direct address with name or familiar relationship.
- This is not a glossary substitution rule — each instance needs a sentence-level recasting judgment. The correct approach varies by context: direct name, "you" + role, or restructuring the line to avoid the label.

This pattern applies to all XX家的 / X房的 constructions that function as third-person identification or address in family-dialogue scenes. The same restriction applies in narration: when a character is described by their branch or household position (e.g. "the second branch's wife"), recast using the character's name or a natural relational description rather than preserving the literal branch label. Stage 5 hard gates (pipeline, quality gate, consistency check) passed "Second Branch" without flagging it — this is a known literary-review gap that automated gates do not catch.

This restriction applies to both adult and minor characters. Descriptors like "三房的秦明宝" must be recast as "Mingbao Qin, the youngest granddaughter" or simply "Mingbao Qin" — never as "the third branch's young daughter." The branch label must not survive in its literal nominal form.

## Kinship address terms (三婶, 二嫂, 大伯, etc.)

This rule extends the household-address policy above to all generational kinship labels. Chinese family hierarchy is structurally too complex to map cleanly onto English — "Third Aunt," "Second Sister-in-Law," "Eldest Uncle" produce unnatural fiction and require readers to track a clan structure they have no way to visualize.

Policy: resolve all kinship address terms to the character's established name.
- 三婶 / 三弟妹 → Lady Gu
- 母亲 (when used by Liuxi Qin addressing Lady Wang) → Lady Wang
- 父亲 (when used addressing a named character) → resolve to that character's established name
- Add further resolutions to `titles_and_terms.md` as new characters are encountered

This applies in both address and narration. Do not preserve the generational label even as a secondary gloss.

## Game-rank and internet-culture idioms

Chinese terms from gaming, internet slang, or modern competitive culture (e.g. 青铜 meaning "low rank/novice," 王者 meaning "top-tier/ace," 青铜/王者 as a contrast between amateur and expert) must be translated by cultural function, not by surface literal meaning:

- Identify the real-world function: 青铜 here signals "amateur/low-ranked," 王者 signals "top-tier/expert." Translate that function.
- Do not render 青铜 as literal "bronze" when the context is about skill ranking or perceived incompetence. This produces awkward English ("they thought she was bronze") that readers without Chinese gaming culture will find opaque.
- Prefer natural English equivalents that preserve the contrast: "They had taken her for a novice, but she was a master," or "they thought they were dealing with an amateur — turns out she was the expert." Match the scene's register (casual/serious/humorous).
- If the term is embedded in dialogue (e.g. a character using gaming slang), consider whether that slang would plausibly exist in the character's world. If not, translate the comparative function rather than the gaming term.
- When in doubt, prefer clarity of the scene's point (novice vs expert, amateur vs ace) over literal fidelity to the gaming metaphor.

## Technical exposition in fiction prose

Medical, Daoist, alchemical, formula, or other specialized explanations (symptom lists, formula breakdowns, procedural descriptions) must remain accurate but must not read as textbook exposition:

- Do not enumerate clinical symptom lists in flat narrative voice ("vomiting blood, bloody stools, sudden chills and shivering accompanied by high fever, a crimson tongue, a thin, rapid, barely perceptible pulse"). This stops the fiction and switches register to a medical textbook.
- Embed specialized information into scene action: a character listing symptoms while examining a patient, a physician mentally running through a formula, dialogue where one character explains to another.
- Preserve the information fidelity but reshape the delivery so it stays in the fictional register: shorter phrases, embedded in observation, varied sentence rhythm.
- Character knowledge should sound like that character thinking or speaking, not like a reference work narrating at the reader.
- This is most important for long runs of technical content (formula reasoning, diagnosis, ritual procedure). Break them into scene beats with character reactions, interruptions, or physical actions.
- If the source itself is a dense block of technical exposition, the translator's job is to find the scene within it — do not simply convert the Chinese technical terms into English technical terms and call it done.

## Daoist / qi-perception vocabulary

Metaphysical-perception nouns (黑气, 煞气, 怨气, 灵气, 妖气) name an omen the Daoist-aware character perceives, not a literal physical substance. Translate by function in the scene (death-omen, ill aura, dark sign), not by morpheme. Weak smell: literal-substance calques such as "black vapor / evil steam / spirit gas." See gold example #3.

## Physical / figure description

Stock figure and feature descriptors (凹凸有致, 楚楚动人, 风韵犹存, 柳叶眉, 杏眼) translate by overall impression, not part-by-part. Weak smell: phrase-by-phrase calques such as "chest and waist distinct," "pitifully lovely," labeled-inventory feature lists. Preferred direction: one or two natural English clauses carrying the impression at the source's evaluative angle (admiring / pitying / appraising), without amplification.

## Chapter-title refrain

This book reuses chapter titles inside the chapter as short narrative refrains. When the source restates or near-restates the chapter title, render the refrain as an isolated short paragraph in the same casing and punctuation as the heading. Weak smell: the refrain buried inline mid-paragraph, lowercased to look like ordinary narration. See gold example #5.

## Compact interior compounds

Two- or three-element interior-state compounds (不满与莫名烦躁, 又惊又喜, 半是欣慰半是怅惘) translate to one short clause carrying the dominant feeling, with the secondary shading as a verb, adverb, or follow-on beat. Weak smell: three-modifier piles such as "a mix of dissatisfaction and confused irritation" or "a complicated blend of joy and surprise." See gold example #4.

## Ritual gesture rendering

Chinese court and household ritual gestures carry gender, rank, and cultural specificity that "bowed" or "nodded" erases. Preserve the cultural form:
- 福礼 / 福了一礼 — feminine salute, hands clasped at the side with a slight dip. Not a bow. See glossary entry.
- 万福 — standard feminine greeting gesture. Do not flatten to "bowed."
- When the specific gesture is underdetermined, prefer "performed a formal salute" over generic "bowed."

This is not a substitution rule. Apply sentence-level judgment.

## Chapter number formatting

Render chapter numbers as spelled-out ordinals: "Chapter Fourteen," not "Chapter 14." Matches the literary register of the prose. Do not mix numeral and spelled-out forms within the same manuscript once a form is established.

## Anachronistic modern medical metaphors

Avoid modern medical or scientific metaphors (e.g., 'shot of adrenaline,' 'chemical reaction') in narrative prose set in a historical/fantasy Chinese world. When the source uses a metaphor that is itself medical but culturally grounded (强心针 = 'tonic for the heart'), prefer historical equivalents ('reviving tonic,' 'cordial,' 'stimulant draft') rather than modern equivalents ('adrenaline,' 'IV drip'). Match the register of the setting. See also §"Modern loanwords."

## Acupoint names in medical scenes

When acupoint names appear in medical scenes (针刺, 施针), preserve the pinyin name but add a minimal contextual gloss for the reader (e.g., 'the Zhiyin acupoint on the foot,' 'the Hegu point on the hand'). One- or two-word glosses are enough — do not over-explain. Do not replace pinyin with English translations (e.g., do not render 至阴 as 'Reaching Yin').

## Modern loanwords in semi-historical settings

Avoid modern loanwords (e.g., "schadenfreude," "déjà vu") in narrative prose of a historical/fantasy Chinese setting. If the source describes gloating or wry amusement (e.g., 幸灾乐祸), use native English expressions ("barely concealed glee," "wry delight") rather than foreign loanwords. This restriction does not apply to established world-building terms (e.g., "karma," "dantian") that function as integrated vocabulary within the genre.

## Inner voice tone — direct vs literary

When the source uses a short, blunt inner-voice line (e.g., `不高兴`, direct negation), match the bluntness. Prefer one- or two-word sentence fragments like *Unhappy.* / *Not happy.* over literary paraphrases like "She was displeased" — those flatten the character's authentic, unvarnished inner voice.

This applies in particular to Liuxi Qin's internal reactions: she is direct and unvarnished in her thoughts. A terse inner-voice line should read as a terse inner-voice line, not as a polished narrative description.

In sarcastic dialogue openers (e.g., `喲`), preserve the biting, sarcastic tone — do not soften to neutral 'Oh.' The character's sarcasm should retain its edge; the question-mark variant ('Oh?') can help signal the derisive inflection.

## Narrative self-reference ('your granddaughter' / 'your son' etc.)

When a character refers to themselves by their relation to the addressee (孙女 → 'your granddaughter', 侄儿 → 'your nephew', 弟子 → 'your disciple'), preserve this formal, deferential register. Do not replace with plain 'I'. The relational self-reference marks social hierarchy and politeness. Use natural English: 'your granddaughter,' 'your son,' 'your disciple.' Avoid overly literal constructions like 'this granddaughter.' Applies to both narration and dialogue when the original explicitly uses this structure. See gold example #10.

## Time unit register

When the source uses traditional Chinese time units (时辰, 刻, 更), preserve the historical register:
- Prefer 'the hour of the ox,' 'the third watch,' 'a quarter hour' when the exact time is not critical
- If exact conversion is possible (一个时辰 = 2 hours), render as '2 hours' only when precise duration matters to the plot
- Do NOT convert to modern 24-hour clock times or bare modern units like 'sixteen hours' — these break the historical register
- Exception: 一刻钟 = 'a quarter hour' per glossary (fixed measurement)

## Earthly-branch / zodiac time units

When the source uses 时辰 or earthly-branch time (午时, 申时, 亥时 etc.), prefer natural English equivalents such as 'midday hour,' 'mid-afternoon hour,' or 'the third watch' rather than literal animal-zodiac forms ('the hour of the horse'). If the exact time is plot-critical and the zodiac hour is the only explicit marker, retain the animal form *and* gloss it parenthetically on first occurrence in the scene (e.g., 'the hour of the monkey — the mid-afternoon — had come'). The goal is readability for an English YA audience, not preservation of Chinese horary terminology.

## Inner-voice coarse language (他娘的 etc.)

When a character uses coarse language in inner voice (这他娘的, 他妈的 etc.), do not over-translate the vulgarity. The source's register is often wry, reluctant, or grudging rather than angry or profane. Prefer mild expletives ('Hell,' 'Blast,' 'Damn') over strong ones ('Fuck'). Colloquial but not vulgar. Match the character's overall inner-voice register: Ying Nan's inner voice is tense and self-deprecating, not furious.

## Sarcastic / self-deprecating inner voice (Liuxi Qin's comic fragility)

When Liuxi Qin uses exaggerated self-deprecation or comic fragility (e.g. 我身体不好 used as a joke to avoid work), do NOT translate the surface literal meaning ('I'm not in good health') — this sounds like a genuine claim and loses the character's wry, sarcastic tone. Preserve the comic hyperbole: 'my health is too fragile,' 'with my constitution,' 'in my delicate state.' The register is ironic, not factual.

## Master-Disciple grumbling register (孽徒 / 欺师)

When a master internally grumbles about a disciple (孽徒, 欺师), preserve the gruff affection and wry irritation. Do not escalate to 'unruly,' 'defaming,' or legalistic language. Preferred equivalents: 'that wretched disciple,' 'making a fool of me,' 'taking advantage of the master.' The register is affectionate complaint, not accusation.

## Child character whining / dialogue register (呜, 哇, 呀)

When a child character produces a whining sound (呜-, 哇, 呀), determine whether the source treats it as spoken dialogue or internal thought. If spoken aloud, render as an introductory narrative beat ("[Child] gave a choking whimper") followed by quoted speech. Do NOT collapse into internal thought. Do NOT over-modernize the interjection. The register is childish but natural, not internet-cute.

## 防我 vs 隐瞒 register

When the source uses 防我 ('guarding against me'), do NOT render as 'keeping something from me' (隐瞒). The former is about defensive posture and mistrust — the other party has their guard up against the speaker. The latter is about withholding information. These are distinct emotional stances. Correct equivalents: 'being guarded against' / 'on guard against him/her.' Incorrect: 'keeping something from me' / 'hiding something.'

## Inner voice register — Ying Nan's wry self-deprecation

Ying Nan's inner voice when reacting to Liuxi Qin's accurate prophecies uses short, blunt, wry phrases — often a brief denial or dark-humor observation (e.g., 'No blood yet. Doesn't count.'). Keep the register terse and self-mocking, not expanded into full sentences. Use period-separated short clauses. Italicize inner thoughts. Do not soften the dark humor.

## Inner voice register — Liuxi Qin's exasperated or rhetorical thoughts

Liuxi Qin's inner voice when exasperated or wry often uses rhetorical questions (不自觉？能行？etc.) with a casual, slightly complaining tone. Match the register to wry, colloquial, slightly dramatic but not childish. Prefer 'Can I afford not to?' over 'How can I afford not to?' — the former is more natural and less formal. The difference is small but tonal.

## Chapter-title compression

Do not compress or drop elements of chapter titles. Two-action headers must preserve both actions (e.g., 结交巴结秦神医 → 'Befriending and Currying Favor with the Divine Physician'). If the source title has two coordinated strategies or verbs, the English must preserve both. A single collapsed verb ('Seeking Favor') flattens the author's chosen structure.

## 鄙人 humble self-reference

When a character uses 鄙人 in dialogue, preserve the formal, deferential register. Do not flatten to plain 'I' in every instance. Prefer 'this humble one' or equivalent deferential phrasing, especially in formal or hierarchical dialogue (e.g., a merchant/gentry character addressing a Daoist). When 鄙人 appears multiple times in close succession, vary naturally between 'this humble one' and 'I' with formal register cues.

## 'Old lady' reference specificity

When 老太太 / 老夫人 appears in scenes with multiple households, resolve to the specific character name (e.g., 'Old Lady Qian', 'Old Lady Qin') rather than leaving it as generic 'old lady.' The generic form causes ambiguity in multi-household scenes.

## Kinship address — 李叔 (Uncle Li)

When characters refer to 李叔, resolve to 'Uncle Li' in dialogue. This follows the pattern for 李婶 (Aunt Li) and other senior-servant titles. Do NOT render as the surname alone ('Li') which loses the familiar senior-servant register.

## Kinship address — 大嫂 resolution

大嫂 (elder brother's wife) in dialogue or narration must resolve to the character's established name (Lady Wang). Do NOT render as 'Sister-in-law' (ambiguous) or 'Elder Sister-in-law' (overly literal). This follows the existing kinship-address policy: the kinship label is replaced by the character's name.

## Child-character register in dialogue and narration

When a child character (e.g. Mingchun Qin) speaks or is described, preserve the source's register: short, hesitant sentence fragments, slight self-awareness, and vulnerable but not sentimental interiority. Do NOT over-exaggerate childlike speech ('tummy,' 'widdle') — breaks the literary register. Do NOT flatten to fully adult speech — loses the character's age and vulnerability. The right register is 'small but not cutesy; vulnerable but not whining.' Prefer concrete sensory details (stomach gurgle, small bites, red eyes) over sentimental language.

## Daoist address register — 善人

When a Daoist addresses a layperson as 善人 ('good sir' / 'kind sir'), preserve the formal register. Note that 善人 in the context of Daoist teaching or veiled warning carries an ironic or pointed edge — it is not a neutral pleasantry. Do not soften the surrounding sentence to a polite general remark; the weight and directness of the source must survive.

## Modern idioms and proverbs

Avoid importing modern Western idioms (e.g., 'curiosity killed the cat') into historical/fantasy Chinese settings. When the source uses a modern-sounding idiom (好奇害死猫), prefer neutral English equivalents ('curiosity leads to trouble,' 'curiosity is dangerous') over known Western catchphrases that break the world's register. This applies even when the Chinese source itself borrowed the phrase from English — the translation should render the function, not re-import the Western original.

## Paragraph duplication prevention

Verify that the translation does not contain near-identical repeated passages within the same output. If the source paragraph appears once, the English must also appear once — not accidentally duplicated due to a revision artifact or segmentation boundary. During review, check for accidental paragraph repetition especially near chapter or passage breaks. A repeated paragraph is a critical error that breaks narrative flow.

## Scene completeness and truncation

Ensure the translation covers all paragraphs of the source passage. Do not skip introductory paragraphs that establish mood, character voice, or scene context. A truncated translation loses narrative flow, character-establishing beats, and the source's full register. This applies especially to short inner-voice lines, lazy or informal character beats (e.g., wiping drool, picking up a snack), and the narrator's explicit address-system annotations — these are the passages most frequently dropped when a model silently compresses content.

## Face-reading / physiognomy terminology

When the source uses specialized face-reading terms (e.g. 子女宫 'children's palace,' 命宫 'fate palace,' 夫妻宫 'marriage palace'), preserve the technical term with a brief contextual gloss the first time it appears in a scene, or recast the sentence to convey the face-reading meaning without literal calque. Do not flatten or ignore the term — these details characterize Liuxi Qin's Daoist perception and are plot-significant. The reader should feel she is reading a face, not simply looking at one.

## Onomatopoeia and sound-effect formatting

When the source uses isolated onomatopoeic words on their own line (噗通, 咚, 啪, etc.), render them as isolated italicized one-word paragraphs with a terminal period (e.g. *Plop.* / *Thud.* / *Slap.*). Do not bury sound effects inline or omit them. This matches the narrative's rhythmic use of sound to mark actions and should remain consistent throughout the manuscript.

## Daoist name vs title distinction

When a character has both a given name and a formal Daoist name (e.g., Teng Zhao / Xuan Yi 玄一), preserve both identity markers in the translation. Do not substitute the Daoist name with a generic title like "Disciple." The source distinguishes them for a reason — honorific, sect identity, and scene context. Render the Daoist name where the source uses it, with title if contextually appropriate (e.g., "Xuan Yi" or "Disciple Xuan Yi," not "Disciple Teng Zhao").
