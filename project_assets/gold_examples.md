# Gold Examples (Taste Anchors)

This file collects positive taste anchors: short source fragments paired with renderings that match the project's literary instinct on this book.

## What this file is

- A small, growing set of **example pairs** (source → preferred English rendering) that capture the *feel* of a correct translation choice.
- Each entry includes a one-line note on **why it works** and, when useful, what kind of weaker rendering it avoids.

## What this file is *not*

- Not a glossary. Glossary entries are stable mappings; gold examples are situational.
- Not a style rule. Style notes (`4. style_notes.md`) state principles; gold examples show what those principles look like landed in real prose.
- Not a bad-cases list. Bad-case notes catalogue what to avoid; gold examples show what to aim for.
- Not a global find-and-replace. The same source phrase in a different register, speaker, or scene may legitimately render differently.

Treat each entry as a **taste anchor**, not a rigid rule. When a future passage's situation matches, the gold rendering should be a strong default — but the translator/reviewer is still responsible for fit.

## When to consult this file

- During Prompt B review: if a phrase or line feels close to a recorded anchor, check whether the candidate rendering matches the anchor's instinct.
- When a previously translated phrase reappears and the context is genuinely similar.
- When proposing a "creative" rendering that drifts from a recorded anchor — confirm the drift is intentional, not accidental.

If a candidate rendering reaches the same effect through a different surface form, that is allowed. Anchors define the *target sensibility*, not the only acceptable wording.

## Update rule

- Add an entry only when a rendering clearly captures the right feel and you'd want future agents to see it.
- Keep each entry short: source fragment, preferred rendering, one-line "why", optional one-line "weak rendering avoided".
- If a later, stronger rendering supersedes an entry, update in place rather than accumulating near-duplicates.
- If two entries genuinely conflict, move the unresolved tension to `5. unresolved_decisions.md` and leave a pointer here.

## Entries

### 1. `啧啧暗叹`

- **Source:** `啧啧暗叹`
- **Preferred rendering:** *clicked his tongue inwardly* (or *gave a silent click of the tongue* in close-narrated contexts)
- **Why it works:** preserves the small, involuntary, almost-audible quality of the gesture, and keeps it interior — the character is reacting to himself, not performing for anyone. The physical *click* carries the narrator's wry texture without commentary.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** flattening to *sighed inwardly*, *muttered to himself*, or *shook his head in admiration* — these lose the specific tongue-click image and replace texture with generic interior reaction.

### 2. `你长得有点丑，不像我和你弟弟。`

- **Source:** `你长得有点丑，不像我和你弟弟。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *You're a bit ugly. Not like me and your brother.*
- **Acceptable variant:** *You're rather ugly. Not like me and your brother.* — preserves bluntness; "rather" is a slightly stronger hedge than "a bit" but still lands. This variant is also the form echoed back in chapter 3 as a refrain (`Rather Ugly.`), so within this book it is in-canon.
- **Why it works:** keeps the bluntness intact, lets the second sentence land as its own small blow, and trusts the reader to feel the cruelty without softening modifiers. The short, plain English mirrors the unembellished Chinese, and the line break between clauses preserves the casual, almost throwaway delivery that makes the cut sharper.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** smoothing to *You're not as good-looking as your brother and me*, *You're a little plain compared to us*, or any version that hedges *丑* into "plain" / "not as handsome". The point of the line is the bluntness; softening it neutralizes the scene. Also avoid *Nothing like me or your brother* for the second clause — it over-hardens 不像 into a categorical denial, where the Chinese is a quieter comparative.

### 3. Daoist-perception 黑气 (death/ill omen on a face)

- **Source pattern:** `脸上的黑气更重了` / `面上隐隐有黑气` and similar — a Daoist-aware character perceiving an aura on a body.
- **Preferred rendering:** *the death-pall on her face had deepened* / *a darkening sign clung to her face*. Choose the English noun by what the perception **means** in the scene (death, illness, anger, ill omen).
- **Why it works:** treats the term as a perception of meaning, not as a physical phenomenon. The English reader receives the omen the way the observing character does — as a sign — without the literal-vapor image that breaks the world.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *the black vapor on her face had grown heavier*, *black gas/steam/smoke around her face*. These are calques of the morpheme rather than the function and read as converted text.

### 4. Compact interior compound (e.g. `不满与莫名烦躁`)

- **Source pattern:** Two or three short interior-state nouns joined into one compound feeling.
- **Preferred rendering:** name the dominant feeling in one short clause and let the secondary shading sit as a verb, adverb, or follow-on beat — e.g. *her brows knit, displeased and obscurely irritated.* Two beats; not three.
- **Why it works:** matches the source's compactness. The unsorted, half-named quality of the feeling is part of the characterization; preserving it preserves the texture.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *her brows knitting together with a mix of dissatisfaction and confused irritation* / *a complicated blend of X and Y* — three or more modifiers piled on one noun. Reads as over-translated; explains a feeling the source deliberately leaves un-explained.

### 5. Chapter-title refrain (e.g. `雪上加霜` re-appearing inside the chapter)

- **Source pattern:** the chapter title (or near-restatement) reappears inside the chapter as a short narrative refrain — often a single phrase or interior thought at a moment of recognition.
- **Preferred rendering:** isolate it as its own short paragraph, in **Title Case with a terminal period and no quotation marks** — e.g. *Adding insult to injury.* on its own line. Title Case + period applies even when the source uses sentence-final `！` or wraps the line in 引号; the refrain is narrative callback, not spoken dialogue, so source `！` and quote marks do not carry over.
- **Why it works:** preserves the structural callback. The refrain is doing the same work the chapter title does, and isolating it (and casing it like a heading) lets the reader feel the echo.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *adding insult to injury* buried inline mid-paragraph, lowercase, with no separation. Loses the refrain function and reads like a stray idiom.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** rendering the refrain as quoted dialogue with sentence case and an exclamation mark — e.g. `"Rather ugly!"` for the ch3 echo of gold #2. The quotes reframe a narrative callback as a spoken line; the lowercase + `!` reframes a heading-style refrain as ordinary speech. Entry #2's `Rather Ugly.` is this anchor applied to the ch1 cruel-line echo in ch3 and is the canonical surface form for that refrain.

### 6. `忽地悲从心来`

- **Source:** `忽地悲从心来`
- **Preferred rendering:** *a wave of grief washed over her* / *grief welled up from within*
- **Why it works:** Captures the involuntary, unbidden quality — grief that rises up rather than being expressed outward. "Washed over her" keeps it as something happening to the character, not something she performs.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *she felt a wave of grief* (adds deliberateness the source doesn't have); *grief surged through her* (too dramatic for the scene's quiet register).

### 7. `屋漏偏逢连夜雨` (distinct from `雪上加霜`)

- **Source:** `这可真是屋漏偏逢连夜雨了。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Misfortune never comes alone.*
- **Why it works:** Captures the relentless piling-on — not a single second blow, but misfortune arriving in multiples. Reads cleanly as a narrative line without the rain/water imagery that would feel odd in prose.
- **Distinction to hold:** 雪上加霜 → "adding insult to injury" (gold #5). These are different idioms with different weights. Do not use either rendering for the other.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *adding insult to injury* (that is 雪上加霜, not this idiom); *when it rains, it pours* (acceptable but weaker — loses the sense that misfortune travels in company).

### 8. Stakes under-translation — `还得有些准备`

- **Source:** `还得有些准备` (midwife in a difficult labor scene)
- **Preferred rendering:** *you must prepare for the worst*
- **Why it works:** The Chinese phrase is understated in surface form but carries maximum weight in context. "Some preparation" is literally accurate but collapses the stakes. When a source phrase is hedged by convention but means the worst, the English must carry the real meaning, not the surface hedge.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *some preparation* / *be a little prepared* — these read as routine caution, not as a warning that life is at risk.

### 9. Explanatory background passage — `秦家出事来得突然…`

- **Source:** `秦家出事来得突然，导致抄家时都来不及藏些什么，非但没能从家中带出什么值钱东西，就连身上的钗环锦裳都被摘了，不然女眷们不会只穿着简单的素裙里衣。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *The Qin family's disaster had come so suddenly that during the raid there was no time to hide anything. Not only had they been unable to bring any valuables from home, even the hairpins and brocade garments on their bodies had been stripped away — otherwise the women would not be wearing only simple plain underskirts.*
- **Why it works:** Holds an explanatory, rueful tone without becoming clinical. Uses concrete detail (hairpins, brocade garments) and natural phrasing ('stripped away'). The em-dash creates the right narrative pause. Trusts the reader to feel the indignity without editorializing.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** Flat enumeration or overly clinical register that converts the passage into background exposition rather than narrative scene.

### 10. Formal self-reference — `祖母，孙女…`

- **Source:** `祖母，孙女已让人准备了姜汤和热水`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Grandmother, your granddaughter has already had ginger soup and hot water prepared.*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the formal, deferential register of 孙女 without sounding stilted. 'Has already had ... prepared' is natural and idiomatic. This is the model for handling Chinese relational self-reference terms: preserve the form, render in natural English, do not flatten to 'I.'
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Grandmother, I've already had ginger soup prepared* — grammatically fine but loses the social register that '孙女' carries in formal family address.

### 11. Karmic language — `结了这个因果`

- **Source:** `吃了人家一口桃花酥，结了这因果，自然是要还的。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *I ate a bite of her peach blossom pastry. That formed a karmic bond. Naturally, I had to repay it.*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the casual yet philosophical tone of Liuxi Qin's voice. 'Karmic bond' is a natural English equivalent for 因果 that fits the world-building and is not over-modernized. The short clause structure mirrors the original's rhythm.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *I ate her peach blossom pastry, creating a karmic cause-and-effect, so naturally I had to repay it* — too wordy, loses the lightness.

### 12. `脸色有几分难看` — grave news register

- **Source:** `脸色有几分难看` (Liuxi after delivering a grim diagnosis)
- **Preferred rendering:** *her expression turned somewhat grave*
- **Why it works:** 'Grave' carries the weight of the situation (bad news, grim diagnosis) without veering into drama. Matches the detached, clinical register Liuxi maintains in medical scenes.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *her expression turned somewhat sour* — reads as petty or childish. Wrong register for a scene with mortal stakes.

### 13. Sarcastic opener — `喲`

- **Source:** `喲，二婶这会儿倒是清醒了`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Oh? So Lady Xie is finally clear-headed now*
- **Why it works:** The question-mark inflection mimics the derisive, backhanded register of 喲. Signals sarcasm without commentary.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Oh, so Lady Xie is finally clear-headed now* — the same words without the '?' read as a neutral observation. The biting edge is lost.

### 14. Parallel death-pledge — `生是秦家媳妇，死是秦家鬼`

- **Source:** `生是秦家媳妇，死是秦家鬼`
- **Preferred rendering:** *I am a Qin daughter-in-law in life, and a Qin ghost in death.*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the parallel structure exactly. 'Qin ghost' is vivid but not over-interpreted — trusts the English reader with the idiom. The defiant, absolute-loyalty register of the original survives intact.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *I will live and die as a member of the Qin family* — correct in meaning but loses the parallel structure and the specific ghost/spirit imagery.

### 15. Onomatopoeia — isolated sound effects (噗通, 咚, 啪 etc.)

- **Source pattern:** `噗通` / `咚` and similar — a single sound-effect word on its own line or beat.
- **Preferred rendering:** *Plop.* / *Thud.* — isolated on its own line, italicized, one word, terminal period.
- **Why it works:** Mirrors the narrative's rhythmic use of isolated sound to mark physical action. The short isolated paragraph preserves the beat; the italics signal sound effect without commentary; the period closes it cleanly.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** burying the sound inline (*"...the coin hit the floor with a plop"*) or omitting it entirely. Both lose the narrative punch and the visual isolation the source uses intentionally.

### 16. Derogatory physician assessment — `哪来的黄绿大夫，瞎说！`

- **Source:** `哪来的黄绿大夫，瞎说！`
- **Preferred rendering:** *What kind of yellow-green quack was this, spouting rubbish?*
- **Acceptable variant:** *What quack was this, talking nonsense?* — drops the color idiom but keeps the contempt.
- **Why it works:** Preserves the contemptuous, dismissive register of Liuxi Qin's internal assessment. 'Yellow-green quack' carries the original's semantic range while staying readable. The rhetorical question and blunt close mirror the source's short, punchy structure.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Where did this unprofessional doctor come from?* — loses the contempt entirely and reads as polite puzzlement.

### 17. Character signature — `她没有抢病人的习惯`

- **Source:** `她没有抢病人的习惯`
- **Preferred rendering:** *She had no habit of stealing patients.*
- **Why it works:** Concise, vivid, and matched to Liuxi Qin's blunt, no-nonsense voice. 'Stealing patients' is natural English for territorial medical competition. The short declarative sentence mirrors the source's rhythm and characterizes her with one clean beat.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *She was not in the habit of competing for patients* — grammatically fine but too soft; loses the specific 'stealing' image and the character's sharpness.

### 18. `狮子开大口` — exorbitant demand idiom

- **Source:** `狮子开大口`
- **Preferred rendering:** *daylight robbery / a blatant grab for money / an outrageous demand*
- **Why it works:** Idiom meaning 'to ask an exorbitant price' (lit. 'lion opening its big mouth'). Translating the function rather than the literal image keeps the emotional register and the crowd's shock intact. 'Daylight robbery' is natural English for the same register of price-gouging accusation.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** literal renderings like 'lion's open mouth' or 'the lion opens its big mouth' — produce opaque English and break the scene's register.

### 19. Functional idiom — `金刚钻`

- **Source:** `有钱，我就是那金刚钻！`
- **Preferred rendering:** *With money in hand, there is no task I can't handle.*
- **Why it works:** Renders the idiom's function (skill/capability to do any job) without the literal 'diamond drill' image. Preserves Liuxi Qin's boastful, confident register in natural English. 'No task I can't handle' matches the source's emphatic, swaggering tone.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *With money, I am the diamond drill!* — opaque and modern-sounding; breaks the historical setting and produces unintentional comedy.

### 21. Daoist salutation — `福生无量天尊`

- **Source:** `福生无量天尊`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Blessings of the Limitless Heavenly Worthy!*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the ritual weight and the Daoist-specific form (Heavenly Worthy = 天尊, not 'Heavenly Lord'). Capitalization and exclamation mark signal a formal ritual exclamation, not a generic blessing.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Blessings be upon you* (too Christian-register); *May the Heavenly Worthy bless you* (too explanatory, loses the exclamatory punch).

### 22. Sarcastic divine parenthetical — Three Pure Ones

- **Source:** *[Internal thought: sarcastic prayer to the Three Pure Ones after something goes badly]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *The Three Pure Ones: Tacit approval? One day I'll come down from heaven and strike her dead.*
- **Why it works:** Matches the source's sarcastic, quasi-divine parenthetical register. The colon after 'Three Pure Ones' mirrors the source's punctuation and creates the right beat. The italics signal internal thought; the question-mark inflection and dry threat 'strike her dead' hit the correct wry-contemptuous register without softening or over-explaining.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** Softening the threat to a vague complaint — the violent specificity is the point.

### 23. Idiom insult — `磨刀石磨出来的嘴`

- **Source:** `你这张嘴，是用磨刀石磨出来的吧`
- **Preferred rendering:** *That mouth of yours — did you whet it on a grindstone?*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the idiom and the sarcasm. 'Whet it on a grindstone' is a natural English equivalent that maintains the sharpening image. The rhetorical question format matches the source's exasperated, slightly admiring complaint.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Your tongue is very sharp* — correct in meaning but loses the wry, specific image.

### 24. Childlike onomatopoeia — `肚子咕噜的一声响`

- **Source:** `肚子咕噜的一声响`
- **Preferred rendering:** *His stomach let out a gurgle.*
- **Why it works:** Captures the small, involuntary sound without adding cuteness or editorial comment. 'Gurgle' is specific and natural; the sentence stays in the scene's register without performing the childlike register.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *His stomach gave a rumble* (too loud/dramatic); *His tummy went grumble* (too childish/cutesy).

### 25. Child dialogue — short hesitant claim

- **Source:** `我吃饱了，不吃了。能留着吗？`
- **Preferred rendering:** *I'm full. I won't eat any more. Can I keep it?*
- **Why it works:** The three short declarative sentences mirror the source's childlike rhythm — self-assertion, self-correction, tentative request. The hesitancy in the last question ('Can I keep it?') conveys the child's vulnerability without sentimentality. No softening modifiers; the bluntness is the register.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *I've had enough — may I keep the rest?* — grammatically adult, loses the fragmented rhythm of a small child negotiating.

### 26. Humble deflection — `言重了，我只略懂此术`

- **Source:** `言重了，我只略懂此术`
- **Preferred rendering:** *You flatter me. I only know a little of this art.*
- **Why it works:** 'You flatter me' is a natural English equivalent for 言重了 (lit. 'your words are heavy/excessive'). 'A little of this art' preserves the understatement and the specialist register without over-literalism. Liuxi Qin's modesty here is polished and controlled, not self-deprecating — the rendering matches that quality.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *You're too kind. I just know a bit about this* — loses the specialist 'art' framing; *I'm not as skilled as you suggest* — over-explains the deflection.

### 27. Deferential address in male-disguise — `不知如何称呼公子`

- **Source:** `不知如何称呼公子`
- **Preferred rendering:** *I hardly know how to address you, young master.*
- **Why it works:** Captures the deferential, puzzled-but-respectful register of Squire Qian addressing Liuxi Qin in male disguise. 'I hardly know how to address you' preserves the courteous helplessness. 'Young master' is the established rendering for 公子 when used in this context (see titles_and_terms.md).
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *How should I call you?* — too casual; loses the formal, deferential weight.

### 28. Emotional color — face going pale

- **Source:** `钱员外夫妻两个脸色白了`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Squire Qian and his wife both went pale.*
- **Why it works:** Clean and idiomatic. 'Went pale' is the standard English equivalent for 白了 in emotional-response contexts. The dual subject 'Squire Qian and his wife' handles the source's paired reference naturally.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Squire Qian and Lady Qian's faces turned white* — 'turned white' is slightly clinical; keeps the literalness of the color without idiomatic fluency.

### 29. Sudden physical dread — `钱员外心里打了个寒颤`

- **Source:** `钱员外心里打了个寒颤`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Squire Qian felt a chill run through him.*
- **Why it works:** Vivid, controlled, and idiomatic. 'A chill run through him' is the natural English equivalent for a sudden cold shudder of dread — matches the emotional register exactly without literalism or over-explanation.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Squire Qian's heart shivered with cold* (literal calque); *Squire Qian was filled with sudden fear* (explains the emotional result instead of rendering the sensation).

### 30. Terse vindictive inner voice — `活该`

- **Source:** `活该` (inner voice, after witnessing someone's comeuppance)
- **Preferred rendering:** *Serves him right.*
- **Why it works:** The blunt, unsympathetic two-word English matches the terse, unvarnished inner register exactly. No softening, no elaboration. This is the gold standard for Liuxi Qin's inner voice when it's satisfied by someone else's misfortune.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *He deserves it* (slightly formal, loses the snap); *Serves you right* (second person, shifts address).

### 31. Emphatic deliberate repetition — `看我愿不愿`

- **Source:** *if they really do, it depends on whether I'm willing to give them that chance — and whether I'm willing at all*
- **Preferred rendering:** *If they really do, it depends on whether I'm willing to give them that chance — and whether I'm willing at all.*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the deliberate, emphatic repetition of the source structure. The em-dash creates the right pause before the second beat, and the repetition of 'whether I'm willing' is intentional, not redundant — it mirrors the source's rhetorical construction.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *whether I agree* (loses the emphatic register); collapsing the two 'willing' beats into one verb clause.

### 32. Wry satisfaction inner voice — `竟还得了两个功德`

- **Source:** `没想到，钱家还不错，竟还得了两个功德`
- **Preferred rendering:** *The Qian family turned out pretty well. I even managed to gain two merits from it.*
- **Why it works:** 'Even' perfectly captures the mildly surprised, satisfied quality of 竟 — the speaker didn't expect it but is pleased. 'Turned out pretty well' for 还不错 is natural and appropriately understated. Short declarative sentences mirror Liuxi Qin's wry inner register.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *It turned out the Qian family was quite good, and I unexpectedly gained two merits* — over-explains the surprise; loses the casual, satisfied inner-voice rhythm.

### 33. Visual scan into observation — Concubine Wan's rough cotton

- **Source:** *[Liuxi Qin scans, then processes what she sees]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *She lifted her head and saw Concubine Wan, dressed in rough cotton, bent over her needle and thread.*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the source's visual scan, the internal observation, and the wry affect of the perceiving character — all in one clean compound clause. The specific detail (rough cotton / needle and thread) grounds the visual in the scene without editorializing.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** Over-describing the scene or explaining Liuxi Qin's reaction; both break the controlled observational register.

### 34. Observational pause — roughened hands

- **Source:** *[Liuxi Qin observes Concubine Wan's hands, pauses on the detail]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *Liuxi Qin looked down at the woman's hands — visibly roughened in these short days — and nodded.*
- **Why it works:** The em-dash creates a brief pause that mirrors the source's observational beat — the hands are seen, the observation registers, then the response. 'Visibly roughened' is precise and unembellished. The trailing 'and nodded' closes the beat without elaboration.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** Omitting the observational pause; wrapping the detail into a plain clause ('her roughened hands') loses the beat structure.

### 35. Tears forming — `眼眶里有些热`

- **Source:** `王氏的眼眶里有些热`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Heat welled in Lady Wang's eyes.*
- **Why it works:** Elegant and natural. 'Heat welled' is idiomatic English for the sensation of tears forming — captures the physical precursor without saying 'she was about to cry.' Precise and controlled.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Lady Wang's eyes turned red* (different physical description); *tears formed in Lady Wang's eyes* (jumps past the sensation to the result).

### 36. Near-tears observation — `泫然欲泣`

- **Source:** *[Lady Wang sees Mingchun's barely suppressed emotion]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *After Liuxi Qin had gone, Lady Wang saw the barely suppressed tears in Mingchun Qin's eyes and the little-boy hurt on his face. A rare smile crossed her own.*
- **Why it works:** 泫然欲泣 is a literary descriptor for near-tears. 'Barely suppressed tears' is the natural English equivalent. 'Little-boy hurt on his face' adds the emotional color without over-translating. The maternal observation in the final beat ('a rare smile crossed her own') lands cleanly.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *with tears about to fall* (literal); *on the verge of tears* (too generic for the literary register of the source).

### 37. Wry self-characterization — `万婉柔天生就是这材料`

- **Source:** *[about Wan Wanrou avoiding danger instinctively]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *Avoiding danger was human instinct — and Wan Wanrou was a natural.*
- **Why it works:** 'A natural' is sharp, colloquial, and retains the slightly humorous self-report without being flat or literary. The em-dash creates the right comedic pause before the punchline. The register is wry and self-aware, matching the source's tone.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Wan Wanrou was naturally good at avoiding danger* — grammatically correct but loses the wry punchline quality; states the observation instead of landing it.

### 38. Household address resolution in dialogue — `大太太`

- **Source:** `我把这家中之事交予大太太`
- **Preferred rendering:** *household matters go to Lady Wang*
- **Why it works:** Clean, natural instantiation of the kinship-address resolution rule. Does not preserve the literal household-rank label ('First Madam'); resolves directly to the character's established name. Flows naturally in dialogue.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *leave household matters to the First Madam* / *entrust it to the first branch's wife* — both preserve the label and produce unnatural fiction.

### 39. Casual dismissal of scheming — `别让她们给嚯嚯了`

- **Source:** `别让她们给嚯嚯了`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Don't let them ruin it.*
- **Why it works:** Captures the colloquial, dismissive register of 嚯嚯 without over-translating or sounding modern. Matches Liuxi Qin's direct, no-nonsense voice. Short imperative sentence mirrors the source's bluntness.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Don't let them wreck it* (too casual/modern); *Don't let them spoil it* (too mild — loses the specific flavor of 嚯嚯 which implies active damage).

### 40. Deferential old-servant voice — `这老奴从不做这种事`

- **Source:** *[Nanny Ding speaking to Liuxi Qin about not spreading information]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *Nanny Ding gave a wry smile. 'This old servant would never do such a thing. I have only been watching out for you.'*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the deferential, loyal register of the old servant with 'this old servant.' The wry smile and the 'only been watching out for you' create the right complex of deflection and loyalty. Natural English in the right register.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *I would never do such a thing* — dropping the self-referential form loses the social register; *This humble servant* — overly formal for the intimate household relationship.

### 41. Direct reassurance — Liuxi Qin's arrangements voice

- **Source:** `不用担心。我把这个给你，已经想好了`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Don't worry. Since I'm giving this to you, I've already made the arrangements.*
- **Why it works:** Short declarative sentences mirror Liuxi Qin's direct, no-nonsense tone. 'I've already made the arrangements' is natural and carries the authority the source implies. No hedging, no warmth performance — just reassurance through competence.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *You needn't worry. I've thought it through and have already taken care of everything* — over-explains; the source trusts the listener to fill in the implied competence.

### 50. Defensive posture — `对方在防我`

- **Source:** `齐骞心里却有种对方在防我的感觉。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Qi Qian felt she was being guarded against him.*
- **Why it works:** Preserves the specific emotional register: the other person has their guard up against the speaker — defensiveness, not secrecy. Short, direct, and true to the source's wry observation of an interpersonal dynamic.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *she was keeping something from him* — shifts from defensive posture to information-withholding, which is a different (and stronger) accusation that is not in the source.

### 49. Ying Nan's inner voice — wry self-denying thought

- **Source:** `应南吞了吞唾沫，没事，他没流血，还不算准。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Ying Nan swallowed. No blood yet. Doesn't count.*
- **Why it works:** Short, blunt, period-separated clauses mirror the source's terse inner rhythm. The wry denial (he's trying to reassure himself that Liuxi Qin's prophecy hasn't come true) is self-mocking without explanation. Italicized formatting marks internal thought cleanly. Minimal words, maximum character voice.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *He told himself that since he hadn't bled, her prediction wasn't accurate yet* — over-explains and loses the terse, wry quality.

### 45. Proverb + reluctant agreement — `宁可信其有，不可信其无`

- **Source:** *[Qi Qian yielding to Liuxi Qin's judgment after the proverb]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *As the saying goes, better to believe it might be true than to assume it won't be. Faced with Liuxi Qin's blunt and decisive judgment, Qi Qian went along with her.*
- **Why it works:** Idiomatic and colloquial rendering of the proverb; 'went along with her' captures the slightly reluctant, practical yielding without making Qi Qian look weak or deferential. Natural English that does not over-translate.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *He believed her* — too simple; *He submitted to her judgment* — too formal and hierarchical.

### 46. 冷不丁 (caught off guard) + polite flustered response

- **Source:** *[Huo Lang receiving unexpected praise from Liuxi Qin]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *Huo Lang, caught off guard by the praise, was delighted. He clasped his hands and said, 'Doctor Qin is too kind.'*
- **Why it works:** 'Caught off guard' is the natural English for 冷不丁. 'Too kind' renders 说笑了 (lit. 'you jest') as a polite, flustered deflection — natural in this register. 'Clasped his hands' preserves the ritual gesture without over-literalism.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Huo Lang was suddenly delighted* — loses the 'off guard' quality; *'You flatter me'* — too Western in register.

### 47. Time-unit idiom — `一盏茶功夫`

- **Source:** `还没到一盏茶功夫，一声雷鸣炸响，然后豆大的雨点噼里啪啦，急促而猛。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *In less time than it takes to drink a cup of tea, a thunderclap exploded, and then rain the size of beans came lashing down, frantic and heavy.*
- **Why it works:** 'In less time than it takes to drink a cup of tea' is a natural English equivalent for 一盏茶功夫 that preserves the classical time register. 'Rain the size of beans' preserves the vivid image. 'Frantic and heavy' captures the paired descriptor 急促而猛 cleanly.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Within minutes* — breaks the historical register; *beans-sized rain* — awkward calque.

### 48. 笑眯眯 (beaming / mischievous smile)

- **Source:** `秦流西笑眯眯的。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Liuxi Qin beamed.*
- **Why it works:** 'Beamed' captures the full-faced, eyes-crinkling, slightly mischievous smile that 笑眯眯 describes. Short, vivid, and aligned with Liuxi Qin's playful-deflecting character register.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *smiled amiably* — too polite and neutral, loses the knowing quality; *smiled warmly* — too earnest.

### 42. Wry self-deprecation — frail constitution as licence

- **Source:** `我体弱矫情`
- **Preferred rendering:** *I have a frail constitution, so I have to be a bit demanding.*
- **Why it works:** Captures the wry, self-deprecating register without over-translating. 'Frail constitution' for 体弱 is natural and slightly formal, matching Liuxi Qin's deliberate diction. 'Be a bit demanding' for 矫情 preserves the tone of playful, deliberate fussiness — she is making a joke, not a complaint. The structure (short, declarative, comma-implied pause) mirrors the source's delivery.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *I'm frail, so I get to be picky* — too casual; *Because of my delicate health, I must insist* — too formal and loses the wry edge.

### 43. Single-word dismissal — `无所谓`

- **Source:** `无所谓`
- **Preferred rendering:** *"Whatever."*
- **Why it works:** Perfectly captures Liuxi Qin's nonchalant, blasé dismissal. The single word in English mirrors the source's one-word reply. No softening, no elaboration — her indifference is total and deliberate.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *"It doesn't matter."* — grammatically equivalent but loses the shrug-register; *"I don't care."* — overly direct, loses the casual quality.

### 44. Affectionate caregiving — rest-and-recover register

- **Source:** *[Liuxi Qin instructing a patient to rest and look after herself]*
- **Preferred rendering:** *You just rest up and take care of that belly of yours.*
- **Why it works:** Sweet, natural, and affectionate without sentiment. 'Rest up' is colloquial and caring; 'that belly of yours' adds specificity and warmth without being saccharine. Matches the source's chipper, no-nonsense register — practical advice dressed in affection.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *Please rest and take care of yourself* — too generic, loses the warmth and the body-specific focus.

### 20. Physical description — 飘逸 + 雌雄莫辩

- **Source:** `那少年纤细高挑，尚未及冠，发束丝带，飘逸随性，五官与画中相符……肤色冷白细腻，雌雄莫辩。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *The young man was slim and tall, not yet of capping age, his hair tied simply with a silk ribbon, giving him an air of untethered ease. His features matched the portrait — long brows slanting into the temples, narrow eyes tilting upward at the corners, clear facial contours, cold-white skin fine and delicate, ambiguous in gender.*
- **Why it works:** 'Untethered ease' for 飘逸 is inspired — preserves the ethereal quality without literal calque. The dash after 'portrait' creates a natural inventory of features. 'Ambiguous in gender' handles 雌雄莫辩 cleanly and trusts the reader.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** 'floating elegance' or 'drifting grace' for 飘逸 — too literal; 'androgynous' for 雌雄莫辩 — modern clinical register, breaks the scene.

### 51. Terse aside — `她不喜欢被打扰睡觉`

- **Source:** `她不喜欢被打扰睡觉`
- **Preferred rendering:** *She hated being disturbed while sleeping.*
- **Why it works:** Short, declarative, in voice. Captures Liuxi Qin's irritated-aside register without padding or emotion words. The single sentence on its own line lands as a thought-beat in the prose.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *She did not like being woken up* — too neutral, drains the irritation; *She really hated being disturbed when she was sleeping* — over-padded, breaks the rhythm.

### 52. Compressed fate beat — `五十五岁。时间不多了。`

- **Source:** `五十五岁。时间不多了。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Fifty-five. Not much time left.*
- **Why it works:** Two clipped fragments mirror the source's rhythm and let the implication sit. The terse register keeps the divinatory weight without spelling it out — the reader does the math.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *She was fifty-five, and there was not much time remaining* — explanatory, kills the rhythm; *Fifty-five years old. There isn't much time.* — slightly verbose, the contracted 'isn't' breaks register.

### 53. Divinatory fate-pattern judgment — `子身残`

- **Source:** `她的脉象更预示晚景凄凉，子身残，她自己更是寿不过甲。`
- **Preferred rendering:** *Her pulse foretold a desolate old age — damage in the line of her children, and she herself would not live past sixty.*
- **Why it works:** Treats the phrase as a divinatory fate-pattern judgment, not a literal medical or physical prediction. `子身残` lands as "damage in the line of her children," preserving the source's judgment register without narrowing `子` to sons or turning `残` into bodily injury.
- **Weak rendering avoided:** *her sons would be broken* — unsupported gender narrowing and a literal-physical misread; *her children would be crippled* — too physical; *her descendants would be cut off* — over-strong.
