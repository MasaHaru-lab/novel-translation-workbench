# Titles and Terms

## Working system

### 大小姐
- Chinese: 大小姐
- Working English rendering: Young Lady
- Notes: Keep as “Young Lady” for now. Do not map this mechanically onto British aristocratic forms such as “Lady + given name.” This is a household-status term, not a British courtesy title. In running prose, wording may vary slightly if rank order or family structure needs to be made clearer.

### 嫡母
- Chinese: 嫡母
- Working English rendering: legal mother
- Notes: Rejected alternatives: “principal mother” (too legalistic/unnatural), plain “mother” (loses hierarchy). “legal mother” preserves the legal-wife / principal-wife distinction from concubine-born structure without sounding legalistic. Do not use “principal mother”.

### 长房
- Chinese: 长房
- Working English rendering: main branch
- Notes: Keep stable unless a better clan-system rendering is adopted consistently.

### 嬷嬷
- Chinese: 嬷嬷
- Working English rendering: Momo
- Notes: For now, prefer retaining “Momo” in role names such as “Ding Momo” rather than forcing “Nanny” or “Nurse.” Final treatment should stay aligned with the broader system for household titles and forms of address.

### 光禄寺卿
- Chinese: 光禄寺卿
- Working English rendering: Chief Minister of the Court of Imperial Entertainments
- Notes: Provisional. Official title system needs later review for consistency and readability.

### 贵妃
- Chinese: 贵妃
- Working English rendering: Noble Consort
- Notes: Prefer stable court-rank terminology once adopted.

### 丫头 / 西丫头 / 你这丫头
- Chinese: 丫头 / 西丫头 / 你这丫头
- Working English rendering: Xi’er
- Notes: Canonical rendering is "Xi’er" — preserves the elder-to-younger familiar register while reading naturally in English dialogue. The character's given name serves as the consistent address form. Rejected alternatives: "girl" (too flat, fails register), "little girl" (infantilizing), "Little Xi" (over-Westernized). Do not use any of these rejected forms.

### 清平观
- Chinese: 清平观
- Working English rendering: Qingping Temple
- Notes: Canonical rendering is "Qingping Temple" (not "Qingping Abbey"). Liuxi Qin's temple. Keep stable across all chapters — do not switch between Temple, Abbey, Monastery, or Shrine. "Qingping Abbey" appeared in earlier draft output and must be corrected.

### 老二家的
- Chinese: 老二家的
- Pattern (instantiate, do not emit verbatim): character name (e.g. Lady Xie) or natural English address
- Notes: Household address term referring to the wife of the second son/brother. Literal renderings like "Second Branch" produce unnatural English fiction. Recast the sentence using the character's name (Lady Xie) or a natural English address in dialogue — the address label itself does not survive translation. See style notes §"Chinese household-address terms" for detailed guidance. Discovered in Stage 5 real-model validation (DeepSeek V4 Flash, 2026-05-02): hard quality gates (pipeline, quality gate, consistency check) all passed, but literary review flagged the gap. Accepted fix: Old Lady Qin addresses Lady Xie directly — "You take Concubine Pan and the others to settle the children first."

### 三婶 / 三弟妹 → Lady Gu
- Chinese: 三婶 / 三弟妹
- Working English rendering: Lady Gu
- Notes: Use Lady Gu throughout. Do not render the kinship label literally ("Third Aunt," "Third Sister-in-Law"). Chinese generational hierarchy terms are too structurally complex to carry over; all such terms resolve to the character's name. See style notes §"Kinship address terms."

### 老太后
- Chinese: 老太后
- Working English rendering: Grand Empress Dowager
- Notes: Senior empress dowager. Do NOT use "Elder Empress Dowager" (unnatural). Use "Grand Empress Dowager" consistently.

### 皇后
- Chinese: 皇后
- Working English rendering: Empress
- Notes: Keep stable. Do not render as "Empress Dowager" or other variant.

### 小人作崇
- Chinese: 小人作崇
- Working English rendering: the work of petty men / mischief by petty men
- Notes: Generic plural phrase meaning underhanded sabotage by low characters. Do NOT render as "Petty Villain" — that reads as a character name. The source is a charge of systemic foul play, not a label for one person.

### 漓城
- Chinese: 漓城
- Working English rendering: Li City
- Notes: Fictional city name. Keep capitalization consistent. Do not add tonal marks. Do not invent alternate forms.

### 九玄
- Chinese: 九玄
- Working English rendering: Jiu Xuan
- Notes: Shop or establishment name. Characters return to it and summon a shopkeeper. Treat as a proper name; use pinyin. Do NOT render as "Nine Mysteries" (literal calque, reads unnatural for a place name).

### 老太太 (when referencing Old Lady Qin)
- Chinese: 老太太
- Working English rendering: Old Lady Qin
- Notes: Resolve to the named character whenever the referent is clear and specific. Bare 'Old Lady' is acceptable in narration only when the household context is unambiguously established and no other matriarch is present in the scene. In dialogue, always resolve to the established character name.

### 玉雪肌
- Chinese: 玉雪肌
- Working English rendering: Jade Snow Cream
- Notes: Proper noun for a high-end cosmetic cream. Capitalize as a product name. In narration, 'the Jade Snow Cream' or bare 'Jade Snow Cream.' Do not romanize as 'Yuxueji.'

### 九玄拍卖馆
- Chinese: 九玄拍卖馆
- Working English rendering: Jiu Xuan auction house
- Notes: The auction venue associated with 九玄. Render as 'Jiu Xuan auction house' — lowercase 'auction house.' Do not translate literally ('Nine Mysteries Auction House').

### 孽徒欺师
- Chinese: 孽徒欺师
- Working English rendering: The Ungrateful Disciple Defies the Master / The Disciple Defies the Master
- Notes: Chapter title pattern. Generic, not specific to any named disciple. Do NOT insert a character name (e.g., 'A Teng Zhao Rebelling Against Their Master'). Preserve the generic master-disciple framing.

### 大姐姐 / 大姐 / 长姐
- Chinese: 大姐姐 / 大姐 / 长姐
- English rendering: Elder Sister (in direct address or narrative reference)
- Notes: When used by a younger sibling to address the eldest sister, prefer 'Elder Sister' over 'Big Sister' (too Western/childish). For narrative uses (长姐形象), render as 'elder sister image' or recast naturally. Per kinship-address policy, may also resolve to the character's name (Liuxi Qin) when the context is clearly named.

### 大太太
- Chinese: 大太太
- English rendering: Lady Wang
- Notes: Household address for the first branch's wife. Resolve to 'Lady Wang' per kinship-address policy. Do not render literally as 'First Madam' or 'Eldest Madam.'

### 福生无量天尊
- Chinese: 福生无量天尊
- Working English rendering: Blessings of the Limitless Heavenly Worthy!
- Notes: Standard Daoist salutation. Render with capital letters and exclamation mark in dialogue. Do not use Christian-toned equivalents ('Heavenly Father,' 'God bless'). The register is exclamatory and ritualistic.

### 钱太太
- Chinese: 钱太太
- Working English rendering: Lady Qian
- Notes: Squire Qian's wife. Use 'Lady Qian' per the project convention for married women (Lady Wang, Lady Xie, Lady Gu). Do not use 'Mrs. Qian' — drifts from the established system.

### 钱老太太
- Chinese: 钱老太太
- Working English rendering: Old Lady Qian
- Notes: Squire Qian's mother, senior matriarch of the Qian household. Matches the convention for senior matriarchs (Old Lady Qin). Keep stable.

### 钱员外
- Chinese: 钱员外
- Working English rendering: Squire Qian
- Notes: Wealthy landowner/squire figure. Use 'Squire Qian' as the stable rendering. The source uses 员外 as a respectful address for a wealthy non-noble landowner. 'Squire' fits the register without importing British aristocracy. Do not switch variants.

### 卒中 (medical term)
- Chinese: 卒中
- Working English rendering: sudden stroke / wind-stroke
- Notes: Traditional Chinese medical term for stroke (cùzhòng). Render as 'sudden stroke' or 'wind-stroke' when spoken by a physician in diagnosis; prefer bare 'stroke' for narrator paraphrase. Do not modernize to 'cerebral hemorrhage' or 'CVA.'

### 小秦大夫
- Chinese: 小秦大夫
- Working English rendering: Doctor Qin
- Notes: Squire Qian's respectful address variant for Liuxi Qin as a physician. Render as 'Doctor Qin' per the 大夫 system — do not preserve the 小 modifier as 'Young Doctor Qin.' The 小 is a polite diminutive that does not carry over into the canonical English form.

### 公子 (honorific for skilled young person)
- Chinese: 公子
- English rendering: young doctor / young sir
- Notes: When used as a generic honorific for a young skilled person (e.g. a physician in a professional context), render as 'young doctor' or 'young sir.' Do NOT default to 'young master' — that rendering is reserved for the male-disguise servant address context (Liuxi Qin traveling as 少年郎, addressed by Chen Pi). Keep the two contexts distinct.

### 公子 (male-disguise address)
- Chinese: 公子
- Working English rendering: young master
- Notes: When Liuxi Qin travels in male guise (少年郎), Chen Pi and other servants address her as 公子 (young master). Render as 'young master' in running prose — lowercase, no quotes. This is a situational address shift, not a permanent name change. Do not Westernize as 'Young Master Liuxi' or similar.

### 大夫 (physician title)
- Chinese: 大夫
- Pattern (instantiate, do not emit verbatim): Doctor + surname (e.g. Doctor Zhang, Doctor Gao, Doctor Yang)
- Notes: Render the physician title as "Doctor" followed by the actual surname — Doctor Zhang, Doctor Gao, Doctor Yang. Never "Dr." (abbreviation), never "Zhang Dafu" (transliteration), and never the literal pattern with brackets. The existing project convention for household roles uses functional English titles (Lady Wang, Ding Momo, Aunt Li); matching "Doctor + surname" keeps the system consistent. This is a working choice — see unresolved_decisions for the ongoing discussion of the 大夫 address system.

### 宋医馆
- Chinese: 宋医馆
- Working English rendering: Song Medical Hall
- Notes: The Song family's medical establishment. Render as "Song Medical Hall" — do NOT use "Song Clinic," which has a modern medical-office register and breaks the historical-fantasy setting. Doctor Song (宋大夫) is the resident physician.

### 血光之灾
- Chinese: 血光之灾
- English rendering: a calamity involving bloodshed / an omen of bloodshed
- Notes: Daoist omen idiom warning of violent injury or death involving blood. Do NOT render literally as 'blood accident' or 'blood disaster' — those are morpheme-calques that obscure the idiomatic meaning. Translate by function as a Daoist omen phrase, not as literal description. See also glossary §'大师 (when addressing a Daoist practitioner)' for related Daoist register guidance.
