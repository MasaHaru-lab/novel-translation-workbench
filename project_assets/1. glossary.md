# Glossary

## Working glossary

### 记在名下
- Chinese: 记在名下
- Working English rendering: registered under [someone's] name
- Notes: Keep the formal household / lineage sense. Do not flatten into casual family phrasing.

### 生养之恩
- Chinese: 生养之恩
- Working English rendering: the debt of giving birth and raising [someone]
- Notes: Context-sensitive. Preserve the layered sense of obligation and upbringing.

### 抚养之恩
- Chinese: 抚养之恩
- Working English rendering: the debt of raising [someone]
- Notes: Related to, but not identical with, 生养之恩.

### 冲煞
- Chinese: 冲煞
- Working English rendering: clashing baleful influence
- Notes: Provisional. Needs stable handling across fate / Daoist / metaphysical language.

### 命格
- Chinese: 命格
- Working English rendering: fate pattern
- Notes: Prefer controlled metaphysical wording; do not make it sound like modern self-help language.

### 清贵
- Chinese: 清贵
- Working English rendering: noble refinement
- Notes: Provisional. Preserve status and moral-social texture; avoid flat modern phrasing.

### 太庙祭祀
- Chinese: 太庙祭祀
- Working English rendering: ancestral temple rites
- Notes: Prefer a stable ritual-language rendering across the book.

### 三牲
- Chinese: 三牲
- Working English rendering: sacrificial triad / the three sacrificial animals
- Notes: Ritual term for the three traditional sacrificial animals (ox, sheep, pig). Prefer concise rendering; avoid enumerating the three unless prose context clearly requires it. Do not paraphrase loosely.

### 七窍流血
- Chinese: 七窍流血
- Working English rendering: bleeding from the seven orifices
- Notes: Keep vivid but controlled. Do not embellish.

### 吹枕头风
- Chinese: 吹枕头风
- Working English rendering: whisper in the emperor's ear
- Notes: Translate by function in running prose, not literally.

### 平反
- Chinese: 平反
- Working English rendering: clear the family name
- Notes: In context, may need slight variation, but preserve the sense of formal reversal of injustice.

### 圣眷
- Chinese: 圣眷
- Working English rendering: imperial favor
- Notes: Keep stable. Do not over-modernize.

### 大灃
- Chinese: 大灃
- Pinyin: Dà Fēng
- Working English rendering: Great Feng
- Notes: Fictional dynasty name. 灃 = fēng, not hóng. Never render as "Great Hong" or "Great Hong Dynasty." Use consistently once stabilized.

### 福礼 / 福了一礼
- Chinese: 福礼 / 福了一礼
- Working English rendering: gave a formal feminine salute / made a formal salute
- Notes: Historical feminine ritual gesture (hands clasped at the side), not a generic Western bow. Do not flatten to "bowed." In running prose, wording may vary but must preserve the ritual-gesture sense.

### 官家出身
- Chinese: 官家出身
- Working English rendering: from an official household / from a family of officials
- Notes: Means the family held civil-service/official positions. Do not elevate to "noble lineage" or "aristocratic." The Qin family are officials, not hereditary nobles. Distinguish from noble/aristocratic status.

### 青铜 (game-rank / skill-level metaphor)
- Chinese: 青铜
- Pinyin: qīngtóng
- Register guidance (no fixed canonical — pick by context): amateur, novice, low-ranked player, or "they thought she was nothing"
- Notes: In Chinese internet and gaming culture, 青铜 (bronze) is the lowest competitive tier. When used as a metaphor in fiction (她是青铜？是王者), it contrasts perceived low skill with actual high skill. Do NOT render as literal bronze — this produces opaque English. Translate the comparative function: novice vs expert, amateur vs ace, they thought she was nothing / they had dismissed her. Context determines the natural English equivalent. This is guidance, not a substitution rule.

### 王者 (game-rank / skill-level metaphor)
- Chinese: 王者
- Pinyin: wángzhě
- Register guidance (no fixed canonical — pick by context): top-tier, ace, master, or champion
- Notes: The counterpart to 青铜 in ranking metaphors. Indicates the highest tier of skill or status. When paired contrastively with 青铜, render the pair consistently — pick one register (novice vs ace, amateur vs master) and keep it. Do NOT render as literal king unless the context is explicitly about royalty, not gaming ranks. This is guidance, not a substitution rule.

### 吉祥物
- Chinese: 吉祥物
- Working English rendering: auspicious creature / auspicious symbol / blessed symbol
- Notes: In founding-myth, dynastic, or ritual contexts, avoid modern "mascot" or "lucky mascot." Prefer historical-register wording that preserves the ritual/symbolic weight.
